PART I — THE METHOD

Part I: The Method

The front of the book. How to think before you look anything up. This is where the four Method diagrams live (period-or-reproduction tree, nail types, dovetails, screws). Everything in the form chapters and the back-of-book atlases hangs off the ideas here.

How to use this guide

This guide is built in three parts, and they work together.

  • Part I, the Method (this section), teaches you how to read a piece: the order to look in, and why construction beats style.
  • Part II, the Forms, is thirty chapters on the pieces you will actually meet, each with a fast "rule it out" test and a careful "confirm it" path.
  • Part III, the Reference Atlases, in the back, teaches the skills the form chapters assume: how to identify a wood, a nail, a joint, a finish. When a chapter says "read the dovetails," the back of the book shows you how.

You do not have to read it cover to cover. Find your piece in Part II, run the test, and flip to the atlases in Part III when you need to confirm a detail.

The field process

Real identification follows an order. Experts do not stare and guess; they run a sequence, fast at first and careful only when a piece earns it. Six steps.

  1. Look. What is the form? Start with shape and structure, not style or age. Is it a chair, a chest, a desk, a table? Then narrow: a chest of drawers or a dresser, a sideboard or a buffet. The form chapters in Part II each open with a ten-second test for exactly this.
  2. Rule it out fast. Most candidates die in seconds on one disqualifier. No plank seat with socketed legs, it is not a Windsor. No knee opening, it is not a dressing table. Eliminate quickly so you only slow down for real possibilities.
  3. Read the construction. Once a piece is on the short list, stop looking at the surface and read how it was built. Open a drawer, turn it over, look at the joints, the fasteners, the saw marks, the woods. This is where age lives. (Part III shows you how to read each of these.)
  4. Date by layers, and stack the evidence. No single clue dates a piece. A wire nail, a machine dovetail, a circular-saw arc, each is one layer. Three or more pointing the same way is a conclusion; one is a hint. The period-or-reproduction decision tree below is this habit in one picture.
  5. Compare to look-alikes. Confirm by ruling out the close cousins. Sideboard or buffet or server? Settee or sofa? Each form chapter carries the comparison that matters.
  6. Estimate value, then call it. Judge value as a range and a direction (common and modest, or premium when original), not a fixed price, because region, condition, and a buyer's emotion move money more than a book can. Then commit to a verdict, and be honest about your confidence.

Style is the weakest evidence

Here is the rule that separates a careful eye from a fooled one: style tells you the least about age. A piece can be made in any year to look like any period. Colonial Revival reproductions copied eighteenth-century forms for decades. So style suggests a date; construction proves it. When the look says one thing and the nails and dovetails say another, believe the construction.

Style still matters, for naming the look and the market, just not for proving the year. Use it last, after the construction has spoken.

⚡ RULE IT OUT IN 10

[BOX, outer rail, top — bold, the first thing the eye hits]

Look at the seat and where the legs go. A Windsor has a solid carved wooden plank seat, with the legs socketed straight up into the underside — no apron, no frame, no rails under the seat.

❌ Upholstered, rush, or cane seat? → Not a Windsor. ❌ Legs joined to an apron or a square frame? → Not a Windsor. ✅ Plank seat + legs driven straight into it, splayed out? → Short list. Keep going.

What it is

The Windsor isn't a style — it's a construction system. Everything about it hangs off one idea: a thick solid-wood plank seat that serves as the structural hub. The back spindles socket down into the seat, the legs socket up into it, and the legs splay outward for stability. The back is built from turned spindles and a shaped crest — never the flat rail-and-stile frame of an ordinary chair.

It's one of the great American forms — Philadelphia, New England, and New York were major centers, with the golden age running c. 1740–1820. But here's the field reality for this guide's era: genuine 18th-century Windsors are largely museum-and-auction pieces. What you'll actually meet from 1840 onward is 19th-century continuation and — by a wide margin — Colonial Revival reproductions (c. 1870 to today). So the money question is almost never "is it a Windsor?" It's "is it period, or a revival reproduction?" That call is the rest of this page.

🔍 ON THE SHORT LIST? CONFIRM IT

The defining features (you want most of these):

  • Solid plank seat, usually saddle-shaped (carved hollows for the sitter)
  • Back spindles socketed into the seat — not mortised into a rail
  • Legs socketed into the seat underside, splayed
  • Turned legs, stretchers, and posts
  • A back type from the family below — but never a rail-and-stile frame

The Rocking Chair

also called: rocker, Boston rocker, platform rocker, nursing rocker, Lincoln rocker

1840 ██████████████████ 1940. The American rocker is everywhere from about 1800 on. Type and construction date it.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
a representative rocker, profile so the rockers read. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A rocking chair rocks, either on curved runners under the legs or on a spring platform base. That is the whole gate.

Then ask one question that decides value more than any other: are the rockers original to the chair? Adding rockers to a plain chair was a common alteration, and runners break and get replaced.

What it is, and the family you will meet

"Rocking chair" is a motion, not a single design, so you will meet several types. Know them on sight:

  • Boston and Windsor rockers. Plank or shaped seat, turned spindle back, often painted and stenciled. (Full treatment in the Windsor chapter and the companion article.)
  • Ladderback rocker. Horizontal slats between two posts, usually a woven rush seat.
  • Pressed-back rocker. Oak factory chair, about 1890 to 1910, with a design stamped into the crest by machine.
  • Platform or spring rocker. Rocks on springs over a stationary base, popular from the 1870s. If it rocks without runners touching the floor, it is this.
  • Upholstered parlor rocker (Lincoln rocker). A framed, padded, high-back Victorian rocker.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • The rockers. Check that runner wood, wear, and the joints where runners meet the legs all agree and look old and settled. Fresh cuts or mismatched wood mean added or replaced.
  • Construction. Hand facets and wedged joints and cut nails point earlier; machine-uniform parts, pressed decoration, and wire nails point to the factory era. (Method section, and the period-or-reproduction tree.)
  • The surface. On painted and stenciled rockers, an original decorated surface is the prize, and stripping it destroys value. You have heard this before, which is the point.

Do not confuse it with

Feature seenLikely type
Curved wood runners under the legsBoston, Windsor, ladderback, or pressed-back rocker
Springs over a stationary basePlatform or spring rocker
Stamped design in an oak crestPressed-back rocker, c. 1890 to 1910
Tall upholstered padded back and frameUpholstered parlor (Lincoln) rocker

What hurts the value

  • Replaced or added rockers, or a botched runner repair.
  • Stripped original paint on a Boston or country rocker.
  • Married parts, a top and base that do not agree.

What it is worth, in plain terms

Most country and factory rockers are common and modest, useful and very saleable. Value climbs toward premium with an early hand-made example, original rockers, and an original painted or stenciled surface. Originality, again, does the lifting.

Quick field routine

First, confirm it rocks, on runners or a spring platform. Second, judge whether the rockers are original. Third, name the type from the list above. Fourth, date by construction and protect the original surface.

The Morris Chair

also called: Mission Morris chair, bow-arm or flat-arm Morris chair, adjustable-back chair

1840 ░░░░░░ 1895 ████████ 1925 ▒░ 1940. English Arts and Crafts origins, American heyday roughly 1895 to 1925.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
your own Morris chair, three-quarter, the back support rod visible. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A Morris chair is a lounge chair with an adjustable reclining back held by a visible manual mechanism: a rod, pegs, a notched bracket, or a hinged support you move by hand. Add an exposed wood frame and broad arms.

If the recline is hidden mechanical hardware, that is a recliner. If the back is fixed, it is an ordinary armchair or lounge chair. The visible back-adjustment rod is the Morris chair's signature.

What it is

The Morris chair is the great Arts and Crafts lounge chair, descended from English designs associated with William Morris and made strongest in America from about 1895 to 1925 in Mission and Craftsman furniture. Its identity is structural: an exposed, dominant wood frame, broad flat arms wide enough for a book or a cup, loose seat and back cushions, and a back whose angle you set by hand with a rod dropped into notches, pegs, or a hinged support. American examples are typically quartersawn white oak with a rectilinear silhouette and honest joinery, often exposed through-tenons and wedge keys.

The key habit: read the mechanism, not the upholstery. Cushions and covers are replaced constantly, so they tell you little. The adjustable back and the exposed frame are what make it a Morris chair.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • The visible back-adjustment mechanism, the surest single feature.
  • An exposed quartersawn oak frame, rectilinear, with broad arms.
  • Exposed through-tenons and wedge keys, the Mission construction signature.
  • Date by frame construction and finish, not by the cushions. Machine-uniform reproduction frames and modern fasteners give away later copies. (Run the period-or-reproduction tree.)

A note on makers: Stickley names carry real value, but Mission vocabulary alone is not a Stickley attribution. Many competitors and later revivals use the same look. Do not attribute to Stickley, Limbert, Roycroft, or any maker without a mark, a label, a documented model match, or strong provenance.

What hurts the value

  • Reproductions sold as period Mission, and false Stickley attributions.
  • A broken or replaced back mechanism, or a refinished and stripped oak frame.
  • (Replaced cushions are normal and do not hurt much. The frame and mechanism are what matter.)

What it is worth, in plain terms

A documented Stickley or named maker Morris chair in original oak and finish is premium, sometimes well into it. A generic period Mission Morris chair is desirable but moderate, and later reproductions are common and modest. The frame, the finish, the mechanism, and any real maker mark set the range.

Quick field routine

First, find the mechanism. Visible manual back adjustment, or it is not a Morris chair. Second, read the frame. Exposed quartersawn oak, through-tenons, broad arms. Third, date by frame and finish, ignore the cushions. Fourth, resist the Stickley name without hard proof.

The Wing Chair

also called: wingback chair, fireside chair, easy chair (early)

1840 ██████████████████ 1940. The form is old, from the 1600s, but what you will find is mostly Federal and Colonial Revival. Read the legs.

your own wing chair, three-quarter. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A wing chair has side wings that project forward from the upper back, up around where your head and shoulders sit. That is the defining feature, present even on small or plain examples.

No wings, no wing chair. An upholstered chair without those forward-projecting side panels is an armchair, a club chair, or a lounge chair.

What it is

The wing chair began as a fireside chair: the wings blocked drafts, held warmth around the sitter, and supported the head during reading or dozing. The back is tall, commonly 38 to 50 inches, the wings reach above the shoulders, and the arms usually sweep down from the wing fronts. The frame is most often fully upholstered, though early American, Federal, and revival examples show exposed wood at the legs and stretchers.

Because the upholstery is almost always later, the legs are your clock.

On the short list, date it by the legs

The cover gets replaced; the legs rarely do. Read them.

  • A married or replaced base, or later feet on an old frame.
  • Worn-out structure under the upholstery (frame, springs, webbing), which matters more than the visible cover.
  • (A later, well-done re-cover is expected and does not hurt much. Read the frame and legs.)

What it is worth, in plain terms

A genuine eighteenth-century wing chair with an original frame and good lines is premium and serious. A Federal or Colonial Revival example, which is what you will usually find, is moderate, and a worn twentieth-century reproduction is common and modest. Frame quality, leg period, and structural condition set the range, not the fabric.

Quick field routine

First, confirm the wings projecting forward from the upper back. Second, read the legs against the period table. Third, confirm the period with construction, machine work means revival. Fourth, judge the frame and structure under the cover, not the cover.

The Settee

also called: parlor settee, hall settee, cane-back settee, settee bench

1840 ██████████ 1930 ▒░ 1940. Made since the 1700s, with a strong Victorian parlor run, c. 1840 to 1930.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
your own settee, three-quarter, full width. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A settee is small, upright, and chair-like multi-user seating with a back, usually for two, commonly 42 to 65 inches wide. It often looks like two chairs joined or an elongated chair.

If it is wide, deep, and soft for lounging, that is a sofa. If it is hard, plain, and shallow with an optional back, that is a bench. The settee sits between them: formal, upright, chair-like.

What it is

The settee is parlor and hall seating, lighter and more formal than a sofa. It may be fully upholstered, partly upholstered, or an exposed wood frame with a cane, slat, or spindle back, and it frequently comes as part of a matched parlor suite with side chairs and an armchair. Its strongest American run is Victorian, roughly 1840 to 1930, across Rococo Revival, Renaissance Revival, Eastlake, and Aesthetic Movement, with continuous Colonial Revival production after.

Arms are common but optional, and arms alone do not turn a settee into a sofa. The deciding traits are scale, upright posture, and chair-like frame.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • Confirm scale and posture: narrower than a sofa, more upright, chair-derived.
  • Look for suite context: a matching side chair or armchair strongly supports a parlor settee reading.
  • Date by frame and construction, as with any seating. The visible style (Rococo Revival carving, Eastlake incising) suggests period, but confirm with joinery, fasteners, and finish. Cane and upholstery are commonly replaced.

A carved Victorian parlor settee, especially with its matching chairs as a suite, is desirable, and the complete suite sells for more than the parts. A plain or heavily restored single settee is common and modest. Carving quality, original surface, and suite completeness set the range.

Quick field routine

First, confirm scale and posture: small, upright, chair-like. Second, look for matching suite pieces. Third, date by frame and construction, not the replaced cane or cover. Fourth, separate from sofa (bigger, softer) and bench (harder, simpler).

The Victorian Sofa

also called: sofa, settee (loosely), camelback, Grecian or recamier, Rococo Revival sofa

1840 ██████████ 1910 ▒░ 1940. Upholstered multi-user lounge seating, with the classic Victorian parlor run c. 1840 to 1910.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
your own sofa, three-quarter, full length. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A sofa is wide, deep, upholstered lounge seating for two or more, with a back and usually arms. It is larger and softer than a settee, and harder to mistake once you compare scale.

If it is small, upright, and chair-like, that is a settee. If it is hard and shallow, that is a bench. The sofa is the deep, soft, multi-user lounge piece of the parlor.

What it is, and the Victorian shapes

You will mostly meet Victorian and early twentieth-century examples. Learn the silhouettes:

  • Grecian or recamier, c. 1840s, a scrolled-end couch with one raised end, Empire into early Victorian.
  • Rococo Revival, c. 1850 to 1865, the classic carved Victorian sofa: a serpentine carved crest with grapes, roses, or finger-roll molding, on cabriole legs, often attributed in spirit to the Belter and Meeks tradition (attribute by name only with proof).
  • Renaissance Revival, c. 1865 to 1885, more architectural, with crests, burl panels, and incised lines.
  • Eastlake, c. 1880 to 1900, rectilinear with shallow incised decoration.

On the short list, date the frame, not the cover

This is the sofa's lesson. The upholstery is almost always a later re-cover, so it dates nothing. Turn the piece over and read what does not change:

  • The legs and the carved crest, which carry the style and period.
  • The springs. Hand-tied coil springs on a webbed frame point to the nineteenth or early twentieth century; modern foam and sinuous wire springs point later or to a rebuild.
  • The frame construction, joinery, and fasteners (Method section).

A fresh cover over an old hand-tied, well-carved frame is a period sofa that has simply been reupholstered. A new cover over modern foam and staples is a modern sofa.

What hurts the value

  • A broken-up parlor suite (sofa parted from its matching chairs).
  • Cut-down or married frames, and a rebuild that replaced hand-tied springs with foam.
  • (Reupholstery itself is expected and does not kill value, as long as the frame is sound and original.)

What it is worth, in plain terms

A finely carved Rococo Revival sofa with a sound original frame, especially as part of a suite, is premium. A plain Victorian or early-twentieth-century sofa is moderate to modest, and value rests on the carving, the frame, and suite completeness rather than on the current fabric.

Quick field routine

First, confirm sofa scale: wide, deep, soft, multi-user. Second, read the silhouette for the Victorian style and period. Third, flip it and date the frame, legs, and springs, not the cover. Fourth, check for marriage and look for matching suite pieces.

The Parlor Table

also called: center table (loosely), marble-top table, lamp table, Victorian occasional table

1840 ░░ 1850 ██████████ 1915 ▒░ 1940. The decorative sitting-room table of the Victorian home, c. 1850 to 1915.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
your own parlor table, three-quarter. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

A parlor table is a small-to-medium decorative table, commonly 24 to 36 inches across and 28 to 32 inches tall, made to face the room in a Victorian parlor and hold a lamp, an album, a vase, or refreshments.

If it is large and meant to anchor the center of the room, that is a center table. If it is plain and seating-adjacent, that is a side table. The parlor table is the small, ornamented, room-facing piece of the sitting room.

What it is

The parlor table is Victorian decorative furniture, made roughly 1850 to 1915 for the parlor, sitting room, and front room. Tops are often marble or other stone, or carved, inlaid, or veneered wood, frequently shaped: oval, round, serpentine, or square with cut corners. Bases range from a central pedestal to four cabriole or turned legs. The ornament tracks the era: Rococo scrollwork, Renaissance Revival cartouches, Eastlake incised lines and ebonized accents, and Golden Oak machine carving. It often belongs to a matched parlor suite with the sofa, settee, and chairs.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • Read the style vocabulary for the period (Rococo, Renaissance Revival, Eastlake, Golden Oak), then confirm with construction: machine work, joinery, secondary woods, and fasteners (Method section).
  • Mind the marble. Marble tops are often replaced, recut, or married to a base. Check the fit, the overhang, the edge profile, the underside support, and whether wear agrees. A perfect modern slab on an old base is a marriage.
  • Mind the casters. Replaced casters are extremely common, and a later rubber or metal caster can fool a quick read into dating the whole table too late. Judge the table by its frame and joinery, not by a wheel that has likely been swapped. (We watched the engine itself get fooled by exactly this.)

What it is

The Chest of Drawers

also called: chest, bureau (regional), tall chest, lingerie chest, semainier

1840 ██████████████████ 1940. Made continuously. The form does not date the piece. The construction does.
HERO PHOTO TO COME
your own chest, three-quarter, full height. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

Picture the proportions and the storage. A chest of drawers is taller than it is wide, it is all drawers with no cabinet doors, it has no knee opening, and it is a single case, not two stacked boxes.

If it is wider than tall with a usable dressing top, that is a dresser. If it has a knee opening to sit at, that is a dressing table or a desk. If it is two carcasses stacked, that is a chest-on-chest or a highboy. A plain tall stack of drawers, no doors, no knee hole, is a chest of drawers.

What it is

The chest of drawers is one of the most consistently produced pieces in American furniture, made from about 1700 right up to today. It is vertical storage at its simplest: a single case of drawers stacked top to bottom, the top surface secondary to capacity. In late Victorian and early twentieth-century homes it was usually part of a bedroom suite, paired with a dresser, a bed, and a nightstand.

Because the form barely changed in two hundred years, the form is not your dating clue. This is the piece where you stop looking at shape and start reading construction.

On the short list, date it by construction

Here the method does all the work. Open a drawer and turn the piece around.

  • Dovetails. Pull a drawer and look at the corners. Hand-cut dovetails, uneven and few, point to earlier or shop-made work. Perfectly even, identical machine dovetails point to factory production after about 1860 to 1870. (See the dovetail diagram in the Method section.)
  • Nails and screws. Cut nails suggest roughly 1790 to 1890. Round wire nails mean after about 1890. A Phillips screw means modern, at least in that part. (See the nail and screw diagrams.)
  • Saw marks. Straight saw kerfs are earlier. Circular saw arcs on a secondary surface mean after about 1830, and usually later factory work.
  • Secondary woods and backboards. Different, cheaper woods for drawer sides, bottoms, and backs are normal and honest. A single uniform wood throughout, or plywood, points later.
  • Hardware. Pulls and escutcheons help, but they are the most-replaced part on any chest, so weigh them lightly. Look for extra holes from earlier hardware.

Stack the tells, do not lean on one. This is the same "stack the evidence" habit from the decision tree, just applied drawer by drawer.

Rule it out in 10

Look at the top and the back. A washstand has a top built to get wet (marble, slate, tile, or zinc), a splash back behind it, and often towel bars on the sides or rear. It is sized to stand at and wash, not to store clothes.

If the top is plain wood meant for storage, the back is a decorative mirror frame rather than a waterproof splash, and the case is drawer-dominant, that is a small dresser or a side table. The washstand reads as a washing station first.

What it is

Before indoor plumbing, every bedroom had one of these. The washstand held the pitcher and basin you washed with. Its whole design is about water: a water-resistant top (commonly marble), a raised splash back to protect the wall, towel bars to dry your hands, a single shallow drawer or a drawer over a small cabinet, and a lower shelf or compartment for the chamber pot, soap, and linens. It stands at a comfortable basin height, compact and purposeful.

It is fundamentally a pre-plumbing solution, so its life tracks plumbing's arrival: bedroom first, briefly the kitchen during the transition, then gone, replaced by the bathroom sink.

On the short list, confirm it, even when parts are missing

This is the washstand's special lesson: the basin and pitcher are almost always long gone, and often the marble too, but the evidence stays. Look for:

  • A water-resistant top, or the mounting and discoloration where one sat.
  • A splash back or gallery, or the fastener holes and finish lines where it was removed.
  • Towel-bar mounts on the sides or rear, even if the bars are gone.
  • Water staining, rings, and finish loss concentrated on the top and rear, the fingerprint of real washing use.
  • A storage layout that pairs a small drawer with an open shelf or little cabinet, sized for washing accessories, not clothes.

Read the scars. A piece that has lost its marble and basin still tells you it was a washstand if you know where to look.

Date it

  • Construction first. Joinery, fasteners, saw marks, and finish layering date it more reliably than style. (The Method diagrams apply here too.)
  • Style lags, especially here. Washstands are private bedroom furniture, and rural and Southern examples lag urban fashion by a decade or more. Painted, plain vernacular examples can look older than they are. Do not date on looks alone.
  • The marble is a trap. Marble tops are often added, replaced, or married to a base later. Check that the marble's wear, fit, overhang, and color suit the base before you trust it.

A refined marble-top walnut or mahogany washstand with its original gallery and towel bars sits in the nicer, more desirable range. A plain painted pine vernacular example is common and modest, though charming and very saleable, and there is a steady market for washstands converted to bathroom vanities. Originality of the top and the survival of the splash and towel hardware matter most.

Quick field routine

First, confirm the form. Waterproof top, splash back, towel bars, basin proportions. Read the scars if parts are gone.

Second, separate it from a dresser. Water evidence and washing layout, not clothing storage.

Third, date by construction, not style, and remember the rural lag.

Fourth, judge the marble. Original, or added later.

The Hoosier Cabinet

also called: Hoosier, Hoosier cupboard, baker's cabinet, kitchen workstation, Sellers cabinet, McDougall cabinet, Napanee cabinet

Rule it out in 10

Look for a work surface between an upper and a lower section, and any sign of a flour bin or ingredient fittings.

A Hoosier is not just a tall kitchen cupboard. It is a freestanding workstation. If the piece is only shelves and doors, with no pull-out or built-in work top and no kitchen-workflow fittings, it is a cupboard, a hutch, or a plain kitchen cabinet. Move on.

If it has a work surface plus a flour bin, a sifter, spice racks, or the scars where those once lived, you are on the short list. Keep going.

What it is

The Hoosier cabinet is the original all-in-one kitchen. Before built-in cabinetry, a family bought one freestanding piece that combined storage, food preparation, and a work surface into a single step-saving station. The name comes from Indiana, the home of the Hoosier Manufacturing Company and many competitors, but the form spread nationwide and was widely imitated. Its strongest years run from about 1900 into the 1930s, fading as built-in kitchens took over before and after the Second World War.

You can recognize the workflow built into it. A lower section with deep storage and drawers. A work surface in the middle, often a pull-out, frequently topped in enamel, porcelain, or zinc for a sanitary, wipe-clean prep area. An upper section organized for ingredients, with spice jar racks, small drawers, shelves, and often a tambour (roll-front) door that hides them. The signature feature is the flour bin with a built-in sifter, mounted in the upper section so the cook could measure and sift in one motion. Accessory hardware can include a sugar bin, a bread drawer, a pull-out recipe-card holder, labeled glass jars, and ant-proof casters.

On the short list, confirm it

Look for several of these, not just one:

  • A flour bin or sifter, or clear scars and mounting holes where one was removed.
  • A pull-out or fixed work surface, especially an enamel or porcelain top.
  • Spice jar racks, small ingredient drawers, or a tambour door in the upper section.
  • Accessory fittings: sugar bin, bread drawer, recipe holder, original labeled jars.
  • A maker tag, metal plaque, decal, or branded hardware.

The more of the workstation system that survives, the more confident the call, and the more the piece is worth.

The Hoosier rewards a few specific tells:

  • Material and finish. Earlier examples, roughly 1890 to 1915, often have oak cases with heavier, furniture-like construction. Depression-era and later refinishing can blur this, so read it alongside the hardware.
  • Hardware and patents. Maker plaques, patented sifter and door hardware, and the flour-bin design are strong dating anchors. Patent dates stamped in metal give you a firm "not before" point.
  • Construction. Machine-made case construction throughout is normal and expected here. This is factory furniture, so do not treat machine work as a strike against it the way you would on an early Windsor.
  • Casters and tops. Ant-proof casters and original enamel tops are period-appropriate and add value when original.

The Blanket Chest

also called: six-board chest, dower chest, dowry chest, hope chest, coffer

Rule it out in 10

A blanket chest is a rectangular box with a hinged lid that lifts from the top, opening into one large open cavity.

If the storage is drawers or doors, it is not a blanket chest. If it is built for travel, with a domed or leather-covered body and trunk hardware, it is a trunk. A plain rectangular case you open from the top, into bulk storage, is a blanket chest.

What it is

The blanket chest is the oldest and simplest case form: a box for storing textiles and household goods in volume. The hinged lift lid is the defining feature. Inside is a large open cavity, often with a small lidded till at one end for valuables. The base varies: bracket feet, bun feet, bootjack ends, or a simple board base. Surfaces range from plain to paneled to richly painted, and cedar linings are common. It is domestic, not portable, which separates it from a travel trunk.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • Confirm the lift lid and open cavity, and look for a till inside.
  • Read the construction, which dates it more than anything. A simple six-board chest nailed together is the vernacular base case; a frame-and-panel chest is more refined. Hand-cut dovetails at the corners and cut nails point earlier; machine dovetails and wire nails point later. (Method section.)
  • Read the base. Bracket and bun feet and bootjack ends each carry period and regional character.
  • Read the surface, and read it carefully, because this is where the value lives.

A word on painted chests, and the prize

Many of the best blanket chests are painted, especially in the Pennsylvania German tradition: tulips, hearts, peacocks, distelfinks, and often a name and date. A dower chest, made for a young woman's marriage goods, is as much a cultural artifact as a piece of furniture.

Here is the rule, for the third time in this book and the most important one: original paint is the prize, and stripping it destroys the value. A decorated dower chest taken down to bare wood loses most of what made it worth having. Learn to tell original decoration (paint that has aged and worn in step with the chest, settled into the grain, crazed with the surface) from later applied decoration (paint that sits on top, too crisp, or wears differently than the wood around it). When in doubt, leave the surface alone and ask.

Look for a cover that rolls or curves down to hide the writing surface, and a fitted interior of pigeonholes and little drawers behind it.

  • A writing surface that is either fixed or revealed when the cover opens.
  • Drawers or pedestals below.
  • Patent-style locking or concealment hardware points to specific quality makers, but do not name a maker without a label or a matching patent mark.

Date it

  • Wood and market. The classic American office roll-top is oak, riding the Golden Oak office-furniture wave of roughly 1890 to 1915. Earlier and higher-style cylinder desks appear in walnut and mahogany.
  • Hardware and patents. Patent-dated locks and roll mechanisms give a firm "not before" point. Late Victorian office hardware is machine-made and standardized.
  • Construction. Machine-cut dovetails on the drawers and interior, wire nails on the back, and uniform machined parts all point to the 1870-to-1920 factory window. (Run the dovetail and nail diagrams from the Method section. Hand-cut dovetails and cut nails would suggest something earlier or a repair.)
  • Reproductions exist. Modern plywood, modern slides, and Phillips screws give them away. When unsure, run the period-or-reproduction decision tree. You will keep meeting that tree, which is the idea.

Size, wood, and the cover drive it. A large oak S-roll desk, with a full fitted interior and a working lock, is premium and very collectible. Rule it out in 10

An iron bed is built primarily of metal: cast iron, wrought iron, brass tubing, or steel, with the headboard and footboard made of castings, bent tubing, scrollwork, and finials rather than wood panels.

Here is the trap, and we watched our own software fall into it: a wooden bed with metal side rails is not an iron bed. Nearly every bed has metal rails. If the headboard and footboard are wood, it is a wooden bedstead. The metal has to be the structure.

What it is

The iron bed was the sanitary, fireproof, affordable bed of the late Victorian and early modern home, made strongest from about 1870 to 1940. Cast and wrought iron, brass tubing, and later tubular steel form the head and foot, often with arched or scrolled designs, knobs, rosettes, and finials. Side rails are angle or channel iron with hook-and-eye or bolt attachment. Because the material is metal, the dating logic is completely different from wood: forget species and joinery, and read the metalwork.

Rule it out in 10

A wooden bedstead is a wood-framed sleeping support: a wooden headboard, usually a footboard, and side rails, with a slat, rope, or platform mattress support.

If the head and foot are metal castings or tubing, that is an iron bed. Metal side rails do not change the answer: a wood head and foot make it a bedstead, even with iron rails (this is the companion to the iron-bed lesson).

What it is

The bedstead is the foundational wooden bed, made in every American period. A complete bedstead is more than a loose headboard, it is the full frame. Within the form live the familiar subtypes, and recognizing them is most of the work:

  • Four-poster, four tall corner posts, sometimes for a canopy.
  • Tester and half-tester, with a full or partial overhead canopy frame.
  • Sleigh bed, scrolled head and foot curving outward, Empire and later.
  • Spool and cannonball, turned posts of stacked spools or topped with a ball, common mid-1800s.
  • Panel bed, flat paneled head and foot, the everyday late Victorian and early-1900s suite bed.

On the short list, confirm and date it

  • Identify the subtype from the list, which carries period character (spool and sleigh point mid-1800s, panel beds point late Victorian to early modern suites).
  • Date by construction: joinery on the head and foot, the rail-to-post joint (mortise, bolt, or hook hardware), wood, saw marks, and fasteners (Method section).
  • Beds are often shortened, widened, or rebuilt to fit modern mattresses. Check the rails and joints for alteration.

Clocks: Tall-Case and Shelf

a tall-case clock and a shelf clock, side by side. Wide canvas, generous margin.

Rule it out in 10

Where does it sit? A tall-case clock stands on the floor, a full-height vertical cabinet. A shelf clock sits on a mantel, shelf, or table, compact with a flat base.

That single question sorts the two forms you will meet most.

The one rule that matters most

A clock is two things to date, not one: the case (the furniture) and the movement (the works inside). They can be, and often are, separated, swapped, or married. So date them separately, and never assume the case age proves the movement age or the reverse. The strongest evidence for the movement is inside: paper labels, maker stamps, and patent dates on the works or the case back.

Tall-case (grandfather) clock

A full-height freestanding case with a hood or bonnet, a trunk housing the pendulum and weights, and a base. Weight-driven longcase movements are typical. The term grandfather is later popular usage; the grandmother clock is simply a slimmer, shorter version. Most American tall-case clocks you find are Colonial Revival and Federal Revival, with peak production about 1880 to 1930, not eighteenth-century originals. Date the case by construction and the movement by its label, and watch for a revival case around an older or newer movement.

Shelf and mantel clock

A compact, surface-set, usually spring-driven clock with a decorative front, made to sit on a mantel or shelf. The Victorian American examples are everywhere: black mantel clocks in painted iron or slate, and ornate gingerbread (kitchen) clocks in pressed oak or walnut cases, by makers like Seth Thomas, Ansonia, Waterbury, New Haven, and Gilbert, roughly 1880 to 1920. Tambour clocks, with a low arched silhouette, are an Art Deco favorite, about 1900 to 1940. The maker's paper label inside the case is your best friend for both attribution and date.

A documented period tall-case clock with a matching original movement is premium and serious. A Colonial Revival grandfather clock is moderate. The rule: wood supports a date, it never proves one

A wood species tells you about region, market, and style, and it reinforces a date, but wood alone never dates a piece. Walnut was used in the 1860s and again in 1930s veneer. Oak appears in 1700 and in 1905. Always read wood alongside construction.

Also learn the primary versus secondary wood idea: makers used a good show wood on the surfaces you see (the primary) and cheaper woods inside, on drawer sides, bottoms, and backs (the secondary). The mix of woods is itself a clue. A single uniform wood throughout, or plywood, leans modern.

Reading the surface for age and honesty

  • Crazing and alligatoring, the fine and deep cracking of old shellac and varnish, are honest signs of age and hard to fake well.

  • A stripped or modern-coated surface lowers value, even on a genuine antique.
  • Above all, on anything that might be original-painted or original-surface: leave it alone until you know.

Till — The small lidded compartment inside the upper edge of a blanket chest.
