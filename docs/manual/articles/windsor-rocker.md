# How to Identify and Date an Antique Windsor Rocker

The Windsor rocker is one of the most familiar chairs in American homes, and also one of the most misunderstood. People tend to call any spindle backed rocker a Windsor, and they often assume that an old looking rocker must be worth a fortune. The truth sits somewhere in the middle. Once you know what to look for, you can read one of these chairs in about a minute. Here is how.

## What a Windsor Rocker Actually Is

The word Windsor does not describe a style. It describes a way of building a chair. A true Windsor is built around a solid wooden plank seat. The legs are driven up into holes bored in the underside of that seat, and the back spindles are driven down into the top of it. The legs splay outward for stability, and almost every part, including the legs, stretchers, spindles, and posts, is shaped on a lathe. There is no square frame and no upholstery holding it together. The seat is the hub, and everything sockets into it.

A Windsor rocker is simply that same construction set on a pair of curved rockers. If a rocker has a plank seat with splayed, socketed legs and a spindle back, you are looking at a Windsor rocker. If it has an upholstered or woven seat, or legs joined to a square apron, it is some other kind of rocker, and you can move on.

## The Boston Rocker, the One You Will See Most

By far the most common Windsor rocker you will meet is the Boston rocker, which appeared in the 1820s and was produced in great numbers through the rest of the 1800s. It has a tall spindle back, a seat that rolls up at the back and down at the front, and scrolled arms. The wide top rail, called the crest, was usually painted and often decorated with stenciled fruit, leaves, or gold striping. A lower backed cousin, sometimes called a Salem rocker, follows the same idea. Because these were made by the thousands, an honest Boston rocker is a realistic find at estate sales and in family attics, not a museum only rarity.

## How to Tell Its Age

The real question is almost never whether a chair is a Windsor rocker. It is how old the chair is. Genuine early examples and honest nineteenth century pieces share a handful of tells. Learn to stack them.

Tool marks. Hand work leaves faint facets from a drawknife or a spokeshave, and the parts will not be perfectly identical to one another. Machine made reproductions are uniform and sanded glassy smooth.

Joinery. Older legs and spindles are set with hand fitted joints, often locked with a small wedge. Later pieces lean on dowels, screws, and glue alone.

Wood. Traditional makers chose different woods for different jobs. A soft pine seat that carves easily, hard maple or birch legs, and tough oak or hickory for the bent parts. One uniform wood throughout usually points to factory production.

Paint and surface. Windsor rockers were almost always painted, and Boston rockers were frequently decorated on the crest. An original painted surface with honest, layered wear is the single best thing you can find on one of these chairs.

The rockers themselves. Check whether the rockers are original to the chair. Adding rockers to a plain Windsor chair was a common alteration, and rockers also get replaced over the years. Look for matching wood, matching wear, and old, settled joints where the rockers meet the legs.

No single clue decides it. Three or more pointing toward hand work and age, and you likely have a period piece. A clear machine made tell anywhere, and you should treat the chair as a later reproduction until proven otherwise. Colonial Revival reproductions have been made from the 1870s right up to the present day. Many are charming and useful, but they are decorator furniture rather than antiques, and they are priced accordingly.

## What Hurts the Value

A few common mistakes can turn a good chair into an ordinary one.

Stripping the original paint. This is the most painful error. Sanding a decorated Boston rocker down to bare wood can erase most of its value in a single afternoon.

Heavy replacement of rockers, spindles, or arms. A repair or two is normal on a chair that has rocked for a hundred years, but extensive replacement lowers the value.

Married pieces, where the top of one chair has been joined to the base of another. Make sure the wood, the wear, and the color of age agree all the way around.

## A Quick Field Routine

First, confirm the form. Plank seat, splayed socketed legs, and a spindle back, set on rockers. If those are not present, it is not a Windsor rocker.

Second, study the rockers. Are they original to the chair, or added or replaced later.

Third, decide hand made or machine made, using the tool marks, joinery, and wood.

Fourth, look hard at the surface. An original painted and decorated finish is the prize.

Do those four things, and you can speak about almost any Windsor rocker with confidence.

## When You Want a Second Opinion

If a Windsor rocker has come down through your family and you are not sure what you are holding, that is exactly the kind of question worth a careful, unhurried look. A sound evaluation considers the construction, the surface, and the history together, not just the style. That is the work we love most.
