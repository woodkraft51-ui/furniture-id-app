# Part III: The Reference Atlases

> The back of the book. The skills the form chapters assume. When a chapter says "read the
> dovetails," "read the fasteners," or "read the saw marks," this is where the how-to lives.

---

## How to use the atlases

Part II tells you *what* to look at. Part III teaches you *how to read* it. The form chapters stay fast on purpose, so when one says "open a drawer and read the dovetails," it does not stop to teach dovetails. It sends you here.

Use these atlases two ways. Read them once, front to back, to build the eye. Then keep them as lookups: when a piece is on your short list and you are reading its construction, flip to the right atlas and match what you see against the tables.

Every atlas follows the same shape: the **one rule or warning that matters most**, then a **table of types** with how to tell each one and the date or quality it points to, then a short **how to use it in the field**.

## The atlases in this part

- **Fasteners** · nails and screws, and the date each one pins.
- **Joinery** · how wood is joined to wood: dovetails first, then the rest.
- **Saw marks and tool marks** · what the cutting and shaping left behind, and the era it dates to.
- **Wood species** · the common American furniture woods, primary and secondary.
- **Hardware and casters** · pulls, knobs, hinges, and casters by period.
- **Finishes** · reading the surface, dating the finish, and protecting value.
- **Glossary** · plain definitions of the terms used throughout the guide.

## The one habit to carry into all of them

No single atlas dates a piece by itself. A wire nail, a machine dovetail, a circular-saw arc, a sprayed lacquer: each is one layer. The method from Part I still governs here. **Stack the evidence.** Three layers pointing the same way is a conclusion; one is a hint.
