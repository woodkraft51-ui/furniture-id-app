# The Corner Cabinet
*also called: corner cupboard, corner china closet, corner hutch (in casual use)*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ██████████████████ 1940. Built since the 1700s, common in both early houses and later Colonial Revival interiors.]`

## Rule it out in 10

Look at the **back and the plan**. A corner cabinet is built with a **triangular, canted, or angled back** so it tucks into a room corner. That single fact is the whole identification.

If the back is flat and the piece faces a wall, it is a china cabinet or a cupboard, whatever the tag says. **Corner geometry outranks the seller's vocabulary.** A glazed corner piece is still a corner cabinet, not a china cabinet.

## What it is

The corner cabinet solves a specific problem: storage and display that fits the unused corner of a room. The front may be flat, bowed, arched, or glazed, but the **rear geometry is corner-adapted**, with side boards meeting at an angle and interior shelves often cut triangular or pie-shaped. It can be a built-in fitted to the architecture, or a freestanding case you can move. Upper sections commonly display china behind glass or on open shelves, with a closed cupboard below. A crown or cornice may follow the corner angle.

A warning that comes straight from how these get mislisted: a freestanding corner cabinet **photographed only from the front looks just like an ordinary china cabinet.** Always check the rear and the plan.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm the **angled rear geometry** and any **triangular interior shelves**.
- Decide **built-in or freestanding**. A built-in may share moldings, paint layers, or wall fabric with the room.
- Date by **construction**, as always (Method section). Early built-ins and period freestanding examples show hand work and early fasteners; Colonial Revival reproductions, common from the 1870s onward, show machine work and modern fasteners. When unsure, run the period-or-reproduction tree.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Corner cabinet** | Corner-fit display or storage case | Angled or triangular back, made for a corner |
| China cabinet | Wall-facing glazed display | Flat back, faces a wall |
| Hutch | Two-zone storage plus display | Flat back, base plus a display top |
| Cupboard | General enclosed storage | Flat back, general storage, no corner geometry |

## What hurts the value

- **Marriages and replaced glazing** on a fine early piece.
- **Refinishing** that erases original painted or early surfaces, especially on period built-ins removed from a house.

## What it is worth, in plain terms

An **early period corner cupboard**, especially a fine freestanding one or an architectural built-in with honest old surface, is **desirable and premium**. A twentieth-century Colonial Revival corner cabinet is **common and modest**. Age, surface, and quality of the case set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, check the back and plan. Angled for a corner, or flat for a wall.
Second, decide built-in or freestanding.
Third, date by construction and watch for the Revival reproductions.
Fourth, remember corner geometry beats whatever the tag says.
