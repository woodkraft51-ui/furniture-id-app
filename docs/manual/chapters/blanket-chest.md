# The Blanket Chest
*also called: six-board chest, dower chest, dowry chest, hope chest, coffer*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ██████████ 1900 ▒░ 1940. Made from about 1700 to 1900, the lift-lid storage box of the American household.]`

`[HERO PLATE: your own blanket chest, three-quarter, lid and base feet visible. Wide canvas, generous margin.]`

## Rule it out in 10

A blanket chest is a **rectangular box with a hinged lid that lifts from the top**, opening into **one large open cavity**.

If the storage is drawers or doors, it is not a blanket chest. If it is built for travel, with a domed or leather-covered body and trunk hardware, it is a trunk. A plain rectangular case you open from the top, into bulk storage, is a blanket chest.

## What it is

The blanket chest is the oldest and simplest case form: a box for storing textiles and household goods in volume. The **hinged lift lid** is the defining feature. Inside is a large open cavity, often with a small lidded **till** at one end for valuables. The base varies: bracket feet, bun feet, bootjack ends, or a simple board base. Surfaces range from plain to paneled to richly painted, and cedar linings are common. It is domestic, not portable, which separates it from a travel trunk.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm the **lift lid and open cavity**, and look for a **till** inside.
- Read the **construction**, which dates it more than anything. A simple **six-board chest** nailed together is the vernacular base case; a **frame-and-panel** chest is more refined. Hand-cut dovetails at the corners and cut nails point earlier; machine dovetails and wire nails point later. (Method section.)
- Read the **base**. Bracket and bun feet and bootjack ends each carry period and regional character.
- Read the **surface**, and read it carefully, because this is where the value lives.

## A word on painted chests, and the prize

Many of the best blanket chests are **painted**, especially in the Pennsylvania German tradition: tulips, hearts, peacocks, distelfinks, and often a **name and date**. A **dower chest**, made for a young woman's marriage goods, is as much a cultural artifact as a piece of furniture.

Here is the rule, for the third time in this book and the most important one: **original paint is the prize, and stripping it destroys the value.** A decorated dower chest taken down to bare wood loses most of what made it worth having. Learn to tell **original decoration** (paint that has aged and worn in step with the chest, settled into the grain, crazed with the surface) from **later applied decoration** (paint that sits on top, too crisp, or wears differently than the wood around it). When in doubt, leave the surface alone and ask.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Blanket chest** | Lift-lid domestic storage box | Hinged top, one open cavity, often a till |
| Trunk | Travel storage | Portable build, often domed or leather, trunk hardware |
| Mule chest | Chest with drawers below the well | Lift-lid top over a row of working drawers |
| Six-board chest | Simplest blanket chest | A subtype: six boards, often nailed and painted |

## What hurts the value

- **Stripping or repainting** an original decorated surface. The single worst thing you can do.
- **Replaced feet, lid, or hinges**, or a **married** top and base.
- **Later "folk" decoration** added to plain chests and sold as period.

## What it is worth, in plain terms

A **Pennsylvania German painted dower chest** with original decoration, a name, and a date can be **highly desirable and premium**. A plain six-board pine chest is **common and modest**, though always useful and saleable. Original paint, original surface, and an untouched interior move the value more than anything, every time.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the form. Lift lid, open cavity, maybe a till.
Second, read the construction: six-board or paneled, hand or machine, the fasteners.
Third, read the surface. Original paint is the prize. Do not strip it.
Fourth, check it is honest: original feet, lid, and hinges, no marriage.
