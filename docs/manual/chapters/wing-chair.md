# The Wing Chair
*also called: wingback chair, fireside chair, easy chair (early)*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ██████████████████ 1940. The form is old, from the 1600s, but what you will find is mostly Federal and Colonial Revival. Read the legs.]`

`[HERO PLATE: your own wing chair, three-quarter. Wide canvas, generous margin.]`

## Rule it out in 10

A wing chair has **side wings that project forward from the upper back**, up around where your head and shoulders sit. That is the defining feature, present even on small or plain examples.

No wings, no wing chair. An upholstered chair without those forward-projecting side panels is an armchair, a club chair, or a lounge chair.

## What it is

The wing chair began as a fireside chair: the **wings blocked drafts, held warmth around the sitter, and supported the head** during reading or dozing. The back is tall, commonly 38 to 50 inches, the wings reach above the shoulders, and the arms usually sweep down from the wing fronts. The frame is most often fully upholstered, though early American, Federal, and revival examples show exposed wood at the legs and stretchers.

Because the upholstery is almost always later, **the legs are your clock.**

## On the short list, date it by the legs

The cover gets replaced; the legs rarely do. Read them.

| Legs and feet | Period it points to |
|---|---|
| Turned legs joined by box stretchers, ball or Spanish feet | William and Mary / early Georgian, c. 1690 to 1730 |
| Cabriole legs, pad or trifid feet | Queen Anne, c. 1720 to 1760 |
| Cabriole legs, ball-and-claw feet | Chippendale, mid 1700s |
| Tapered or reeded straight legs | Federal and Hepplewhite, c. 1790 to 1820 |
| Bracket, block, or revival cabriole legs, machine construction | Colonial Revival, c. 1880 to 1940 |

In the 1840 to 1940 window of this guide, most wing chairs you meet are **Federal and Colonial Revival**. Confirm with construction (Method section): machine work and modern fasteners place a chair in the revival era no matter how "early" the legs are styled to look.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Wing chair** | Upholstered chair with forward side wings | The wings on the upper back |
| Armchair | Upholstered or wood chair with arms | No wings |
| Club chair | Low, deep upholstered lounge chair | No wings, lower back |
| Lounge chair | Posture-based relaxed seating | No wings, often reclined |

## What hurts the value
- **A married or replaced base**, or later feet on an old frame.
- Worn-out **structure under the upholstery** (frame, springs, webbing), which matters more than the visible cover.
- (A later, well-done re-cover is expected and does not hurt much. Read the frame and legs.)

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A genuine **eighteenth-century wing chair** with an original frame and good lines is **premium and serious**. A **Federal or Colonial Revival** example, which is what you will usually find, is **moderate**, and a worn twentieth-century reproduction is **common and modest**. Frame quality, leg period, and structural condition set the range, not the fabric.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the wings projecting forward from the upper back.
Second, read the legs against the period table.
Third, confirm the period with construction, machine work means revival.
Fourth, judge the frame and structure under the cover, not the cover.
