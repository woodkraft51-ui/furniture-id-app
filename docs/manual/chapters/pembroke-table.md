# The Pembroke Table
*also called: breakfast table (early), sofa-side table (loosely)*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ░░ then English from 1760, American Federal 1780–1815 ████, Colonial Revival 1890 ██████ 1940 ▒.]`

## Rule it out in 10

A Pembroke table is a **small, refined drop-leaf** with **short hinged leaves on the long sides** and **a drawer in the apron**, light enough to move easily.

If it is a large or plain drop-leaf with no drawer, it is an ordinary drop-leaf. If the leaves are held by swing-leg gates, it is a gateleg. The Pembroke is the compact, drawer-bearing, refined one.

## What it is

The Pembroke is the elegant small table of the late Georgian and Federal years. It has **drop leaves on the long sides** held by brackets or fly rails (not swing-leg gates), **one or more drawers** under the apron, often with a matching **false drawer** on the opposite end for symmetry, and a light, movable body about 27 to 30 inches high. It was used for writing, tea, reading, games, and bedside service. Higher-style examples show **slender tapered or reeded legs, brass casters, inlay, stringing, banding, and figured veneer**, with mahogany dominating Federal and revival pieces. Named in the English tradition for the Earl of Pembroke, it runs from about 1760 in England, peaks in American Federal production around 1780 to 1815, and returns strongly in Colonial Revival, roughly 1890 to 1940.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm the **compact drop-leaf body plus the apron drawer**, the combination that names it.
- Read the **legs and refinement**: tapered or reeded legs, inlay, and stringing point to Federal or to a good revival; confirm which with construction.
- Date by **construction** (Method section). Hand-cut dovetails on the drawer, early fasteners, and honest wear point to the Federal period; machine dovetails and modern fasteners point to Colonial Revival.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Pembroke table** | Compact refined drop-leaf with a drawer | Short side leaves plus an apron drawer |
| Drop-leaf table | Plain hinged-leaf table | Usually larger, no apron drawer |
| Gateleg table | Drop-leaf with swing-leg gates | Swinging gates, not brackets |
| Side table or nightstand | Fixed small table | No drop leaves (later bedside use can confuse this) |

## What hurts the value
- **Replaced leaves, drawer, or hardware**, and a **married** top and base.
- **Lost inlay or stringing**, and refinishing on a fine Federal example.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A genuine **Federal Pembroke** with good inlay, original surface, and crisp construction is **premium**. A **Colonial Revival** Pembroke is **moderate to modest**. The drawer construction, the legs, and the originality of the surface and inlay set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the compact drop-leaf plus apron drawer.
Second, read the legs and refinement for the period.
Third, date by drawer construction and fasteners.
Fourth, check for a marriage and replaced leaves.
