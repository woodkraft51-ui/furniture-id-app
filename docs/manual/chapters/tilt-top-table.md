# The Tilt-Top Table
*also called: tip-top table, snap table, tripod tilt-top, tea table (loosely)*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ░░ then two lanes: original 1730–1800 ████ and Colonial Revival 1880 ██████ 1940 ▒. Silhouette never proves the early date.]`

`[HERO PLATE: your own tilt-top, one image flat and one tilted vertical. Wide canvas, generous margin.]`

## Rule it out in 10

A tilt-top table has a **top that pivots from flat-for-use to vertical-for-storage**, held flat by a **catch or latch** and usually set on a **central pedestal with a tripod base**.

If the top is fixed and does not tilt, it is a plain pedestal table, not a tilt-top. The working tilt mechanism is the defining feature.

## What it is

The tilt-top stood flat for tea or display, then tipped vertical to tuck against a wall and show off its top. Most are pedestal tables on a tripod base, with a turned shaft and three spreading feet. A **catch, latch, wedge, or thumb screw** secures the top down, and many have a **birdcage**, a small block-and-post fitting under the top that also lets it rotate. Tops may be round, square, dished, or carved into a **piecrust** edge.

Two lanes dominate, and telling them apart is the whole game:
- **Eighteenth-century originals**, roughly 1730 to 1800.
- **Colonial Revival and later reproductions**, especially 1880 to 1940 and after.

They can look nearly identical in silhouette, so **form and style alone never prove an eighteenth-century date.**

## On the short list, confirm and date it from the underside

Flip it over. The **underside carries the strongest evidence**: the battens and cleats, the screws and hinge plates, the latch and its wear, oxidation, and the arc-shaped wear where the top has rotated on the birdcage. Read these against the Method section.

- Hand tool marks, irregular hand-cut parts, early fasteners, and deep honest oxidation point to a period piece.
- Machine-uniform turning, modern screws, crisp unworn latches, and clean undersides point to revival.

When unsure, run the period-or-reproduction tree. This is the form it was practically made for.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Tilt-top table** | Pedestal table with a tilting top | Working tilt mechanism and catch |
| Pedestal table | Central-support table, fixed top | Top does not tilt |
| Candle stand | Small tripod stand | Smaller, may or may not tilt |
| Piecrust table | Carved-edge tilt-top | A tilt-top subtype with a scalloped edge |

## What hurts the value
- **A replaced top, birdcage, or latch**, and a **married** top and base.
- **A revival piece presented as eighteenth-century**, the costly mistake.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A documented **eighteenth-century tilt-top**, especially a carved piecrust example, is **premium and serious**. A **Colonial Revival** tilt-top, which is what you will usually meet, is **moderate to modest**. The gap between the two is large, so get the dating right from the underside before you price it.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the top actually tilts and latches.
Second, flip it and read the underside, the real evidence.
Third, decide period or revival with construction and the tree.
Fourth, check top-to-base continuity for a marriage.
