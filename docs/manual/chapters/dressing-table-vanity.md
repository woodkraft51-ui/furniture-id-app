# The Dressing Table and Vanity

## Rule it out in 10

A dressing table has a **knee opening you sit at**, a **grooming surface** at seated height (about 28 to 32 inches), and usually a **mirror** above. Ask the simple question: **can a person sit at it?**

If yes, dressing table. If it is a closed case with no knee opening, it is a dresser or a chest. The seated-use knee opening is the structural difference.

## What it is

The dressing table, or vanity in retail language, is grooming furniture: a seated station with a knee opening flanked by small drawers for cosmetics, brushes, and jewelry, and a mirror, central or triple-fold, mounted above. It comes with a companion stool or bench. The mid-century triple-mirror vanity (about 1920 to 1960) and the Art Deco waterfall vanity are the late, very common examples you will meet.

A crucial caution, and it comes straight from how these get mislisted: **the mirror is often gone, and a vanity without its mirror gets mistaken for a desk.** But the **mirror-mounting evidence usually remains**, the holes, hardware, and vertical supports on the back. When the mirror is missing, let that mounting evidence and the seated-use proportions drive the call, not a guess of "desk."

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm the **knee opening and seated height**, and look for **mirror-mounting evidence** even if the mirror is gone.
- Note the **drawers are accessory-sized**, cosmetics and jewelry, not bulk clothing.
- Date by **construction** (Method section). Victorian walnut and oak point earlier; waterfall veneer and triple-fold mirrors point to the 1920s to 1940s. A matched bedroom suite places it with its dresser and bed.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Dressing table / vanity** | Seated grooming station | Knee opening, seated height, mirror evidence |
| Dresser | Clothing storage case | Closed case, no knee opening |
| Desk | Writing furniture | Writing interior, work proportions, no grooming mirror |
| Washstand | Pre-plumbing washing station | Waterproof top, splash back, towel bars |
| Lowboy | Early dressing-related table | Earlier form on cabriole legs |

## What hurts the value
- **A married or replaced mirror**, and a vanity **separated from its mirror or its suite**.
- **Replaced hardware** and refinishing.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A complete vanity with its **original mirror** and as part of a **suite** is **desirable**. A common waterfall or single vanity is **modest**, useful and very saleable. The original mirror, the hardware, and suite completeness set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, ask: can a person sit at it? Knee opening at seated height.
Second, find the mirror or its mounting evidence.
Third, do not call a mirrorless vanity a desk, read the seated-use proportions.
Fourth, date by construction and suite style.
