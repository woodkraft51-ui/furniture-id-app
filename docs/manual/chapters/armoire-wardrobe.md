## Rule it out in 10

A wardrobe or armoire is a **tall enclosed cabinet with large doors** for storing clothing and household goods, where **door access is the primary function** and the piece is too large to be a mere cupboard.

If it is a chest of drawers, the storage is drawers, not a hanging or shelved cabinet. The armoire and wardrobe are the **big door-fronted clothes cabinets** of the pre-closet home.

## What it is

Before built-in closets, people hung and stored clothes in a freestanding cabinet. The two names overlap, and the difference is mostly degree:

- A **wardrobe** is the simpler, more clothing-functional version: tall, doored, fitted inside with a hanging rod, shelves, or drawers.
- An **armoire** is the broader, more decorative, more architectonic version, often with shaped cornice, carving, paneled doors, or Continental (French, French Canadian, Creole) styling, and frequently used for linens and household goods as well as clothes. A **linen press** is the shelved cousin.

A useful structural tell: many wardrobes and armoires are **knock-down (KD) construction**, built to disassemble into flat panels for moving through doorways. Look for bolts, pegs, or keyed joints at the cornice, sides, and back. That is normal and original, not damage.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm **door-fronted clothes-cabinet** function and read the **interior** (hanging rod, shelves, drawers).
- Check for **knock-down joinery**, which is typical and helps explain the build.
- Date by **construction** (Method section). Continental and fine early armoires show hand work; American Victorian and early-1900s wardrobes are usually machine-made factory pieces in oak or walnut.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Armoire / wardrobe** | Tall door-fronted clothes cabinet | Large doors, hanging or shelved interior |
| Chifferobe | Wardrobe plus a bank of drawers | Hanging side and a drawer side combined |
| Chest of drawers | Drawer storage case | Drawers, not a doored cabinet |
| China cabinet | Glazed display case | Glass doors for dishes, not clothes |
| Cupboard | General enclosed storage | Smaller, general storage, not clothing scale |

## What hurts the value
- **Missing or replaced interior fittings** (rod, shelves, hooks), and **married cornice or doors**.
- **A refinished** fine early or French example.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
Be honest here: large brown clothes cabinets are a **soft market**, because few modern homes need them, so a plain American oak or walnut wardrobe is **common and modest** despite its size. A fine **early or French armoire**, or one with good carving and original surface, is **desirable**. There is also a steady market for large armoires **converted to media or storage cabinets**. Quality, period, surface, and Continental character set the range, not size.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm a tall door-fronted clothes cabinet.
Second, read the interior fittings.
Third, look for knock-down joinery, normal and original.
Fourth, date by construction, and price by quality, not by size.
