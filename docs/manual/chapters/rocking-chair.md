# The Rocking Chair
*also called: rocker, Boston rocker, platform rocker, nursing rocker, Lincoln rocker*

## Rule it out in 10

A rocking chair **rocks**, either on **curved runners** under the legs or on a **spring platform** base. That is the whole gate.

Then ask one question that decides value more than any other: **are the rockers original to the chair?** Adding rockers to a plain chair was a common alteration, and runners break and get replaced.

## What it is, and the family you will meet

"Rocking chair" is a motion, not a single design, so you will meet several types. Know them on sight:

- **Boston and Windsor rockers.** Plank or shaped seat, turned spindle back, often painted and stenciled. (Full treatment in the Windsor chapter and the companion article.)
- **Ladderback rocker.** Horizontal slats between two posts, usually a woven rush seat.
- **Pressed-back rocker.** Oak factory chair, about 1890 to 1910, with a design stamped into the crest by machine.
- **Platform or spring rocker.** Rocks on springs over a stationary base, popular from the 1870s. If it rocks without runners touching the floor, it is this.
- **Upholstered parlor rocker (Lincoln rocker).** A framed, padded, high-back Victorian rocker.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- **The rockers.** Check that runner wood, wear, and the joints where runners meet the legs all agree and look old and settled. Fresh cuts or mismatched wood mean added or replaced.
- **Construction.** Hand facets and wedged joints and cut nails point earlier; machine-uniform parts, pressed decoration, and wire nails point to the factory era. (Method section, and the period-or-reproduction tree.)
- **The surface.** On painted and stenciled rockers, an **original decorated surface is the prize, and stripping it destroys value.** You have heard this before, which is the point.

## Do not confuse it with

| Feature seen | Likely type |
|---|---|
| Curved wood runners under the legs | Boston, Windsor, ladderback, or pressed-back rocker |
| Springs over a stationary base | Platform or spring rocker |
| Stamped design in an oak crest | Pressed-back rocker, c. 1890 to 1910 |
| Tall upholstered padded back and frame | Upholstered parlor (Lincoln) rocker |

## What hurts the value
- **Replaced or added rockers**, or a botched runner repair.
- **Stripped original paint** on a Boston or country rocker.
- **Married parts**, a top and base that do not agree.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
Most country and factory rockers are **common and modest**, useful and very saleable. Value climbs toward **premium** with an early hand-made example, original rockers, and an original painted or stenciled surface. Originality, again, does the lifting.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm it rocks, on runners or a spring platform.
Second, judge whether the rockers are original.
Third, name the type from the list above.
Fourth, date by construction and protect the original surface.
