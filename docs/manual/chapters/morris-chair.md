# The Morris Chair
*also called: Mission Morris chair, bow-arm or flat-arm Morris chair, adjustable-back chair*

## Rule it out in 10

A Morris chair is a lounge chair with an **adjustable reclining back held by a visible manual mechanism**: a **rod, pegs, a notched bracket, or a hinged support** you move by hand. Add an **exposed wood frame** and **broad arms**.

If the recline is hidden mechanical hardware, that is a recliner. If the back is fixed, it is an ordinary armchair or lounge chair. The visible back-adjustment rod is the Morris chair's signature.

## What it is

The Morris chair is the great Arts and Crafts lounge chair, descended from English designs associated with William Morris and made strongest in America from about 1895 to 1925 in Mission and Craftsman furniture. Its identity is structural: an **exposed, dominant wood frame**, **broad flat arms** wide enough for a book or a cup, **loose seat and back cushions**, and a back whose angle you set by hand with a **rod dropped into notches**, pegs, or a hinged support. American examples are typically **quartersawn white oak** with a rectilinear silhouette and honest joinery, often **exposed through-tenons and wedge keys**.

The key habit: **read the mechanism, not the upholstery.** Cushions and covers are replaced constantly, so they tell you little. The adjustable back and the exposed frame are what make it a Morris chair.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- The **visible back-adjustment mechanism**, the surest single feature.
- An **exposed quartersawn oak frame**, rectilinear, with broad arms.
- **Exposed through-tenons and wedge keys**, the Mission construction signature.
- Date by frame construction and finish, not by the cushions. Machine-uniform reproduction frames and modern fasteners give away later copies. (Run the period-or-reproduction tree.)

A note on makers: Stickley names carry real value, but **Mission vocabulary alone is not a Stickley attribution.** Many competitors and later revivals use the same look. Do not attribute to Stickley, Limbert, Roycroft, or any maker without a mark, a label, a documented model match, or strong provenance.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Morris chair** | Adjustable-back Arts and Crafts lounge chair | Visible manual back mechanism, exposed frame |
| Recliner | Mechanical reclining chair | Hidden integrated reclining hardware |
| Lounge chair | Fixed-posture lounge seating | No adjustment, often fully upholstered |
| Armchair | Fixed upright chair | No recline, upright posture |
| Wing chair | Upholstered chair with side wings | Wings on the upper back |

## What hurts the value
- **Reproductions** sold as period Mission, and **false Stickley attributions**.
- **A broken or replaced back mechanism**, or a refinished and stripped oak frame.
- (Replaced cushions are normal and do not hurt much. The frame and mechanism are what matter.)

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A documented **Stickley or named maker Morris chair** in original oak and finish is **premium, sometimes well into it**. A generic period Mission Morris chair is **desirable but moderate**, and later reproductions are **common and modest**. The frame, the finish, the mechanism, and any real maker mark set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, find the mechanism. Visible manual back adjustment, or it is not a Morris chair.
Second, read the frame. Exposed quartersawn oak, through-tenons, broad arms.
Third, date by frame and finish, ignore the cushions.
Fourth, resist the Stickley name without hard proof.
