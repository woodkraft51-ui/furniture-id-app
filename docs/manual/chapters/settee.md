## Rule it out in 10

A settee is **small, upright, and chair-like multi-user seating with a back**, usually for two, commonly 42 to 65 inches wide. It often looks like **two chairs joined** or an elongated chair.

If it is wide, deep, and soft for lounging, that is a sofa. If it is hard, plain, and shallow with an optional back, that is a bench. The settee sits between them: formal, upright, chair-like.

## What it is

The settee is parlor and hall seating, lighter and more formal than a sofa. It may be fully upholstered, partly upholstered, or an **exposed wood frame** with a cane, slat, or spindle back, and it frequently comes as part of a **matched parlor suite** with side chairs and an armchair. Its strongest American run is Victorian, roughly 1840 to 1930, across Rococo Revival, Renaissance Revival, Eastlake, and Aesthetic Movement, with continuous Colonial Revival production after.

Arms are common but optional, and arms alone do not turn a settee into a sofa. The deciding traits are **scale, upright posture, and chair-like frame**.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm **scale and posture**: narrower than a sofa, more upright, chair-derived.
- Look for **suite context**: a matching side chair or armchair strongly supports a parlor settee reading.
- Date by **frame and construction**, as with any seating. The visible style (Rococo Revival carving, Eastlake incising) suggests period, but confirm with joinery, fasteners, and finish. Cane and upholstery are commonly replaced.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Settee** | Small, upright, chair-like seat for two | Narrow, formal, chair-derived frame |
| Sofa | Larger, deeper lounge seating | Wider, softer, lounge posture |
| Loveseat | Small two-seat sofa | Deeper and softer than a settee |
| Bench | Simple elongated seat | Harder, shallower, back optional |
| Settle | High paneled-back seat | Tall enclosed panel back, architectural |

## What hurts the value
- **Replaced cane or upholstery** done poorly, and **refinished** carved frames.
- **A broken suite**, the settee separated from its matching chairs (the set is worth more together).

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A carved **Victorian parlor settee**, especially **with its matching chairs as a suite**, is **desirable**, and the complete suite sells for more than the parts. A plain or heavily restored single settee is **common and modest**. Carving quality, original surface, and suite completeness set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm scale and posture: small, upright, chair-like.
Second, look for matching suite pieces.
Third, date by frame and construction, not the replaced cane or cover.
Fourth, separate from sofa (bigger, softer) and bench (harder, simpler).
