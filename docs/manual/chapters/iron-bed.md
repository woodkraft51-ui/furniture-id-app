# The Iron Bed
*also called: brass bed, metal bed, iron bedstead, hospital or sanitarium bed (institutional)*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ░░ 1850 ██████████ 1940 ▒░. Metal beds run strongest c. 1870 to 1940, with a brass-bed revival from the 1970s.]`

`[HERO PLATE: your own iron or brass bed, head and foot. Wide canvas, generous margin.]`

## Rule it out in 10

An iron bed is built primarily of **metal**: cast iron, wrought iron, brass tubing, or steel, with the headboard and footboard made of **castings, bent tubing, scrollwork, and finials** rather than wood panels.

Here is the trap, and we watched our own software fall into it: a **wooden bed with metal side rails is not an iron bed**. Nearly every bed has metal rails. If the headboard and footboard are wood, it is a wooden bedstead. The metal has to be the structure.

## What it is

The iron bed was the sanitary, fireproof, affordable bed of the late Victorian and early modern home, made strongest from about 1870 to 1940. Cast and wrought iron, brass tubing, and later tubular steel form the head and foot, often with arched or scrolled designs, knobs, rosettes, and finials. Side rails are angle or channel iron with hook-and-eye or bolt attachment. Because the material is metal, the **dating logic is completely different from wood**: forget species and joinery, and read the metalwork.

## On the short list, date it by joining and finish

This is the iron bed's special method.

| Joining method | Period it points to |
|---|---|
| Hand-forged | before 1900 |
| Riveted | industrial, c. 1850 to 1940 |
| Brazed (brass infill at tubing joints) | brass beds, late 1800s onward |
| Oxyacetylene or arc welded | after 1910 |
| Spot welded (tubular steel) | after 1925 |
| MIG or TIG welded | after 1948, a reproduction signal |

Then read the **finish**: japanning (baked black, c. 1850 to 1910), painted (c. 1880 to 1940), brass plating over steel (c. 1880 to 1920, thin and worn through on period pieces), and modern powder coat (post-1960s, a reproduction signal). **Casting seams and file marks** show foundry origin. **Casters** help too: porcelain c. 1860 to 1900, cast iron c. 1880 to 1920. Look for **maker stamps**: Simmons, Foster Brothers, Wickersham, Adams and Westlake.

Reproduction tells: MIG or TIG welds, thick uniform plating with no honest wear-through, and powder-coat finishes.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Iron bed** | Metal-frame bed | Head and foot built of metal castings or tubing |
| Wooden bedstead | Wood-frame bed | Wood head and foot, even with metal side rails |
| Hospital or sanitarium bed | Institutional tubular steel | Crank or removable rails, institutional context |

## What hurts the value
- **Replaced mattress support** (very common, expected, minor).
- **Married head and foot** from different beds, and **modern reproductions** sold as period.
- **Stripped or powder-coated** over original japanning or period paint.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
An ornate **Victorian brass bed** or a decorative cast-iron bed with original finish and brazed period construction is **desirable**. A plain painted iron bed is **common and modest**, and a modern brass-bed reproduction is **modest**. Original finish, period joining, and a maker mark set the range.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the head and foot are metal, not wood with metal rails.
Second, read the joining method against the table.
Third, read the finish and look for casting seams and a maker stamp.
Fourth, watch for MIG or TIG welds and powder coat, the reproduction tells.
