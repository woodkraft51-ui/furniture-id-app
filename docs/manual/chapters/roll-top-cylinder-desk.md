# The Roll-Top and Cylinder Desk
*also called: roll-top desk, cylinder desk, cylinder bureau, tambour cylinder desk, S-roll or C-roll desk*

`[ERA BAND: 1840 ░░ 1870 ██████████ 1920 ███▒ 1940. The form goes back to the 1760s, but the office roll-top you will actually find is late Victorian, roughly 1870 to 1920.]`

`[HERO PLATE: your own roll-top, cover open, fitted interior visible. Wide canvas, generous margin.]`

## Rule it out in 10

Look for a **cover that rolls or curves down to hide the writing surface**, and a **fitted interior** of pigeonholes and little drawers behind it.

If the desk has a flat fixed top, or a flat lid that hinges down to write on, it is a different desk (a fall-front or slant-front). The roll-top and cylinder desk are defined by the moving cover that swallows the whole work surface and locks the clutter away. No moving cover, not this form.

## What it is

This is the desk of the late Victorian office. A flexible slatted cover (a tambour) rolls down on tracks, or a single rigid curved panel (the cylinder) swings down, and the entire fitted interior of pigeonholes, ledger slots, and small drawers disappears behind it, often under one lock. It was the filing-and-privacy machine of its day, made in great numbers in oak from about 1870 to 1920.

Two close relatives sit under one roof here:
- A **roll-top** uses a **flexible cover of many thin slats** glued to a canvas backing, so it rolls into the desk.
- A **cylinder desk** uses a **single rigid curved panel** that lifts or swings as one piece.

Both enclose a fitted office interior. The slats versus the solid curve is how you tell them apart.

## On the short list, confirm it

- A rolling slatted cover, or a rigid curved cylinder cover, with the track, hinge, or slat evidence to show which.
- A fitted interior built for paperwork: pigeonholes, cubbies, small drawers, sometimes a locking system.
- A writing surface that is either fixed or revealed when the cover opens.
- Drawers or pedestals below.
- Patent-style locking or concealment hardware points to specific quality makers, but do not name a maker without a label or a matching patent mark.

## Date it

- **Wood and market.** The classic American office roll-top is **oak**, riding the Golden Oak office-furniture wave of roughly 1890 to 1915. Earlier and higher-style cylinder desks appear in walnut and mahogany.
- **Hardware and patents.** Patent-dated locks and roll mechanisms give a firm "not before" point. Late Victorian office hardware is machine-made and standardized.
- **Construction.** Machine-cut dovetails on the drawers and interior, wire nails on the back, and uniform machined parts all point to the 1870-to-1920 factory window. (Run the **dovetail and nail diagrams** from the Method section. Hand-cut dovetails and cut nails would suggest something earlier or a repair.)
- **Reproductions** exist. Modern plywood, modern slides, and Phillips screws give them away. When unsure, run the **period-or-reproduction decision tree**. You will keep meeting that tree, which is the idea.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Roll-top desk** | Desk with a **flexible slatted** rolling cover | Many thin slats on a canvas backing |
| **Cylinder desk** | Desk with a **single rigid curved** cover | One solid curved panel, not slats |
| Slant-front or fall-front desk | Hinged flat lid you write on | Lid hinges down, no rolling cover |
| Wooton desk | Elaborate cabinet-office desk | Swinging cabinet doors and a dense fitted interior |
| Secretary desk | Desk with a bookcase top | Glazed bookcase above the writing section |

## What hurts the value

- **Replaced or rebuilt tambour slats.** The rolling cover is the heart of the piece. A botched slat repair shows and costs.
- **Reproductions** sold as period.
- **Married tops and bases**, or a cover swapped from another desk. Check that wood, wear, and hardware agree throughout.

## What it is worth, in plain terms

Size, wood, and the cover drive it. A large **oak S-roll** desk, with a full fitted interior and a working lock, is **premium and very collectible**. A small C-roll, or a desk with a damaged or replaced cover, is **common and modest**. A smooth-rolling original tambour and an intact interior are what buyers pay for.

## Quick field routine

First, confirm the form. A rolling or cylinder cover that hides a fitted interior. No moving cover, different desk.

Second, name it. Flexible slats (roll-top) or a rigid curve (cylinder).

Third, date and authenticate. Oak and machine construction for the classic 1870-to-1920 office desk, patent hardware for a firm floor, and the reproduction tells from the tree.

Fourth, test the cover. A smooth, original, complete roll is much of the value.
