# The Dresser
*also called: bureau (regional), standard, double, triple, or princess dresser*

## Rule it out in 10

A dresser is **wider than it is tall**, low enough to use the **top as a dressing surface**, with drawers in **rows or side-by-side banks**, and **no knee opening**. It very often has, or once had, a **mirror**.

If it is taller than wide with a single column of drawers, that is a chest of drawers. If it has a knee opening to sit at, that is a dressing table. Wide, low, broad usable top, multi-column drawers, no knee hole, is a dresser.

## What it is

The dresser spreads clothing storage across width instead of stacking it. Its low, broad top is meant for daily grooming, which is why it so often carries a mirror on a harp or a pair of uprights. It was a core piece of the late Victorian and early twentieth-century bedroom suite, made to match a bed, a chest, and a nightstand. The mirror is frequently long gone, but the **mounting holes and hardware on the back usually remain**, which is your clue that one belonged there.

## On the short list, date it by construction

The form will not date the piece, so read the build, exactly as you did with the chest of drawers. (Same habit, second piece.)

- Pull a drawer and read the **dovetails**, hand-cut and uneven versus machine-even.
- Check **nails** on the back and **saw marks** on secondary surfaces.
- Note the **secondary woods** in the drawer sides and bottoms.
- Look at the **mirror supports**. Original supports and matching wear say the mirror belongs; fresh holes say it was added or swapped.
- Treat the **pulls** lightly, they are the most-replaced part.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Dresser** | Wide, low clothing case with a dressing top | Wider than tall, multi-column drawers, often a mirror |
| Chest of drawers | Tall, narrow drawer stack | Taller than wide, single column |
| Dressing table or vanity | Seated grooming station | Has a knee opening |
| Sideboard | Dining-room storage and serving | Dining context, serving top, not bedroom |

## What hurts the value

- A **married or replaced mirror** that does not match the case.
- **Replaced pulls** that leave the old holes showing.
- **Heavy refinishing** that strips an honest old surface.

## What it is worth, in plain terms

A late Victorian or early twentieth-century suite dresser in oak or walnut is **common and modest**. Value rises toward **premium** with an intact original mirror, original hardware, and a good honest surface. Completeness and originality, as always, do the lifting.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the form. Wide, low, dressing top, multi-column drawers, no knee hole.
Second, look for the mirror or its mounting evidence.
Third, date by construction, not shape.
Fourth, check the hardware and mirror are honest, not married.
