## Rule it out in 10

A china cabinet is a **one-piece glazed display case**, a tall cabinet with **glass doors and usually glass sides**, made to show dishes and china behind glass.

If it is a two-part piece, a storage base with a separate display top, that is a hutch. If its back is angled to sit in a room corner, that is a corner cabinet. The china cabinet is a single unified glass display box.

## What it is

The china cabinet is dining-room display furniture that arrives around 1880, as middle-class households wanted their good china seen. It is a single glazed case, often with **glass shelves**, sometimes a **mirrored back**, and frequently **plate grooves** to stand plates upright. Its golden moment is the Golden Oak era, roughly 1900 to 1915, when the classic American example appears: oak, **curved or bowed glass** on the front and sides, **claw feet** gripping glass balls, and applied carving.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- **Curved or bowed glass** front and sides point to the early 1900s Golden Oak peak. Curved glass is expensive to replace, so an intact original curve is a real plus.
- **Claw-and-ball feet** and applied carving fit the same period.
- **Flat glass** with lighter machine construction can be later, into the Colonial Revival and Depression years.
- Read **construction** as always: machine dovetails on any drawers, wire nails, uniform parts, all consistent with the 1880-and-after factory window.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **China cabinet** | One-piece glazed display case | Single unified glass box for dishes |
| Hutch | Two-zone storage plus display | Storage base with a separate display top |
| Corner cabinet | Corner-fit display case | Angled or triangular back for a room corner |
| Curio cabinet | General display case | Built to show collectibles, not dining china |
| Bookcase | Shelving for books | No plate grooves, shelves spaced for books |

## What hurts the value

- **Replaced curved glass** with flat glass, or cracked original curves.
- **Married tops or replaced feet**, and refinishing that strips the oak.

## What it is worth, in plain terms

An oak **curved-glass china cabinet with claw feet** and intact original glass is **desirable and premium** within this everyday category. A plain flat-glass later cabinet is **common and modest**. The single biggest swing is the curved glass: original and intact lifts it, replaced or broken drops it.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the form. One-piece glazed display case.
Second, look at the glass. Curved and original is the prize.
Third, date by glass, feet, carving, and construction.
Fourth, separate it from a hutch (two-part) and a corner cabinet (angled back).
