## Rule it out in 10

A gateleg table is a drop-leaf whose raised leaves are held up by **swinging gate-like leg assemblies**: hinged legs joined by stretchers that **pivot out from the base** like a gate. Count the legs, often six to eight including the swinging gates.

If the leaves are held by simple brackets or fly rails rather than a swing-out leg-and-stretcher gate, it is an ordinary drop-leaf, not a gateleg. The gate is the whole identification.

## What it is

The gateleg is the dramatic member of the drop-leaf family. When closed it is narrow with the leaves hanging; when open, the gates swing out and the legs march around the perimeter to carry a generous round or oval top. Legs and stretchers are usually **turned**, often barley-twist, ring-turned, ball-turned, or bobbin-turned. The form emerged in late seventeenth-century England, reached American colonial production by about 1700, and was **reproduced heavily in the Colonial Revival**.

## On the short list, confirm and date it

- Confirm the **swing-gate support**, the key identifier.
- Because revival and import copies are everywhere, **early-period attribution must rest on construction, not the Jacobean look**: hinge type, rule and knuckle joints, the **gate-swing wear pattern**, turning style, secondary woods, oxidation, tool marks, and fasteners. (Run the period-or-reproduction tree.)
- Machine-uniform turnings, modern fasteners, and crisp unworn gate joints point to twentieth-century revival.

## Do not confuse it with

| Form | What it really is | The tell that separates it |
|---|---|---|
| **Gateleg table** | Drop-leaf with swing-leg gates | Hinged leg-and-stretcher gates swing out |
| Drop-leaf table | Hinged leaves, fixed supports | Brackets or fly rails, no swing gates |
| Sutherland table | Very narrow drop-leaf | Tiny center, large leaves, gate or swing support |
| Butterfly table | Drop-leaf with wing brackets | Solid butterfly-shaped wing supports, not full gates |

## What hurts the value
- **Replaced gates, leaves, or stretchers**, and re-turned parts.
- **A revival copy sold as period**, the most common pitfall.

## What it is worth, in plain terms
A genuine **early gateleg** with honest wear and original turnings is **desirable and premium**. A **Colonial Revival** gateleg, which is far more common, is **moderate to modest**, useful and attractive but not an antique in the early sense. Construction evidence, not silhouette, sets the range.

## Quick field routine
First, confirm the swinging gate support.
Second, count and read the turnings.
Third, date by construction and gate-wear, not the Jacobean look.
Fourth, decide period or revival with the tree.
