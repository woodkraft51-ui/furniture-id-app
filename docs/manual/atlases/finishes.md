# Atlas: Finishes

> Part III reference. How to read the surface, date the finish, and protect value. This atlas
> carries the most important value lesson in the whole book.

## The lesson, first: the original surface is the prize

Say it plainly, because it is the most expensive mistake people make: **an original old surface is usually the single most valuable thing about a piece, and stripping or refinishing it destroys much of the value.** This is true of painted country furniture, of stenciled rockers, of original-shellac Victorian pieces. When in doubt, **do not strip, do not refinish, ask first.** You can always finish later; you can never get an original surface back.

## The finishes, and how to tell them

| Finish | How to tell it | Period it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Milk or oil paint** | Flat, chalky old paint, often layered and worn into the grain | country and painted furniture, all early periods |
| **Shellac** | Warm amber, dissolves with alcohol, shows fine cracking (crazing) with age | the dominant finish, c. 1820 to 1920 |
| **Varnish (oil)** | Harder, glossier, ambers and alligators (deep crazing) with age | nineteenth century, durable surfaces |
| **Japanning** | Baked black asphalt finish, hard and glassy | early painted metal and some wood, c. 1850 to 1910 |
| **Lacquer (sprayed)** | Even, thin, factory-sprayed, harder than shellac | factory furniture after about 1920 |
| **Polyurethane** | Thick, plastic-like, modern, does not craze naturally | modern and refinished pieces, post-1950s |
| **French polish** | A high, deep shellac shine built in many thin coats | fine formal pieces |

## Reading the surface for age and honesty
- **Crazing and alligatoring**, the fine and deep cracking of old shellac and varnish, are honest signs of age and hard to fake well.
- **Patina**, the soft depth and color that decades of light, oxidation, and handling build, lives on undisturbed surfaces and is destroyed by sanding.
- **Wear in the right places**, edges, arms, handles, feet, should match how a piece is actually used. Even wear everywhere, or none, is suspicious.
- A **thick plastic film** or a flat, fresh, even coat says refinished or modern.

## How to use this in the field
- Identify the finish, then ask whether it is original.
- Honest crazing, patina, and logical wear support age and value.
- A stripped or modern-coated surface lowers value, even on a genuine antique.
- Above all, on anything that might be original-painted or original-surface: leave it alone until you know.
