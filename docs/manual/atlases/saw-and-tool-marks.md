## The rule, first: read the hidden surfaces

Saw and tool marks survive best where no one bothered to finish the wood: drawer bottoms, backboards, the undersides of tops and seats, the insides of cases. **Turn the piece over and look where it was never meant to be seen.** That is where the maker's tools still show, and where a date hides.

As always, a saw mark dates the **board**, not always the whole piece. Repairs and replaced parts carry their own, later marks.

## Saw marks, and the era each points to

| Mark | How to tell it | Period it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Pit-saw / hand-saw** | Straight but slightly irregular strokes, not perfectly parallel, at a slight angle | before about 1830 |
| **Circular saw** | Curved arc marks, evenly spaced, swept by a round blade | American use from the 1830s, common after 1850 |
| **Band saw** | Straight, fine, very even parallel lines | after about 1870 |
| **Modern planer / wide-belt sander** | No saw marks at all; surfaces milled flat or sanded glassy smooth | factory work, twentieth century |

> The single most useful tell: **a curved arc means a circular saw, which means not before the 1830s, and usually later.** A piece styled to look colonial that carries circular-saw arcs is a revival, no matter how early the legs look.

## Tool marks, the shaping evidence

`[VISUAL: diagrams/tool-marks.svg]`

`[OWNER: expand each row; add a macro photo of a drawknife facet vs a sanded-smooth reproduction surface.]`

| Mark | How to tell it | What it says |
|---|---|---|
| **Drawknife / spokeshave facets** | Faint shallow facets where spindles, seats, and legs were shaped by hand | hand work, generally earlier; parts vary slightly one to the next |
| **Hand-plane tracks** | Shallow, slightly uneven scallops across a surface from a hand plane | pre-machine surfacing, or careful shop work |
| **Chisel and gouge marks** | Irregular carving and mortise walls, no two strokes identical | hand joinery and carving |
| **Uniform machine surfaces** | Identical parts, surfaces sanded glassy smooth, no facets | machine reproduction or factory production |

The rhythm is the same tell as joinery: **people are slightly irregular, machines are perfect.**

## How to use this in the field

`[OWNER: tighten to 3-4 lines.]`

Turn the piece over before you decide anything. Find an unfinished secondary surface, raking the light across it so the marks read. Name the saw, name the shaping tool, and add those as layers to everything else: fasteners, joinery, wood, finish, wear. A circular-saw arc or a glassy machine surface is a hard ceiling on age, no matter what the style says.
