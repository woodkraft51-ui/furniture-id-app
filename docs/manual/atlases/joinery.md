# Atlas: Joinery

> Part III reference. How wood is joined to wood, and what each joint says about age and quality.

## Dovetails, the most useful joint

Dovetails lock drawer corners with interlocking wedge-shaped pins and tails. They are the single best place to read a piece, so always pull a drawer.

| Dovetail | How to tell it | What it says |
|---|---|---|
| **Hand-cut** | Few, often uneven in spacing, with thin narrow pins; slight irregularity between them; faint saw and chisel marks | shop-made, generally earlier |
| **Machine-cut** | Many, perfectly even and identical, evenly spaced, with pins and tails the same width | factory, after about 1860 to 1870 |
| **Half-blind** | Visible only from the side, hidden on the drawer front | standard for quality drawer fronts, any period |

The rhythm is the tell: **people are slightly irregular, machines are perfect.**

## The other joints you will meet

| Joint | What it is | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Mortise and tenon** | A tongue (tenon) fits a slot (mortise); the backbone of frame and chair construction | spans all periods; pinned or wedged versions read earlier |
| **Dowel** | Round pegs join two parts | common in later factory work; all-dowel construction leans later |
| **Rabbet and dado** | A stepped or grooved channel holds a panel or shelf | common in case backs and shelves |
| **Rule joint** | A shaped interlocking hinge edge on drop-leaf tables | the mark of a proper drop-leaf |
| **Knock-down (bolted)** | Bolts, pegs, or keys let a piece disassemble | normal and original on big wardrobes and beds, not damage |
| **Biscuit / pocket screw** | Modern shop joints | a modern or reproduction tell |

## How to use this in the field
- Read the dovetails first: even and identical means factory and later, uneven and few means earlier shop work.
- Pinned mortise-and-tenon and wedged joints lean early; all-dowel and biscuit joints lean late.
- Knock-down bolts on a wardrobe or bed are expected and original.
