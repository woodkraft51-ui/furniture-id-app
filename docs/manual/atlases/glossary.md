# Glossary

> Part III reference. Plain definitions of the terms used throughout the guide.

**Apron.** The horizontal frame member below a tabletop or seat, between the legs.

**Bonnet / hood.** The top section of a tall-case clock that houses the dial and movement.

**Bracket foot.** A simple flat foot, shaped on its outer edges, common on case pieces.

**Cabriole leg.** An S-curved leg that swells at the knee and tapers to the foot; Queen Anne and Rococo.

**Case piece.** Any boxlike storage furniture: chest, dresser, cabinet, desk.

**Cornice.** The molded top edge of a tall case piece.

**Crazing.** The fine network of cracks that forms in old shellac and varnish with age.

**Dovetail.** An interlocking wedge-shaped joint used at drawer corners. Hand-cut reads earlier; machine-cut is even and identical.

**Ebonized.** Wood stained or painted dense black to imitate ebony; an Eastlake and Aesthetic trait.

**Escutcheon.** The plate or lining around a keyhole.

**Fall front / drop front.** A hinged panel that drops down to form a writing surface.

**Fly rail.** A pivoting rail that swings out to support a drop leaf.

**Knock-down (KD).** Construction that bolts or keys apart for moving; normal and original on big wardrobes and beds.

**Loper.** A pull-out arm that supports the open lid of a slant-front desk.

**Marriage / married.** Two unrelated parts (a top and a base, a case and a movement) joined to look like one original piece. A value problem.

**Mortise and tenon.** A joint where a tongue (tenon) fits a slot (mortise); the backbone of frames and chairs.

**Patina.** The depth of color and surface that decades of age, light, and handling build; destroyed by sanding.

**Pediment.** The decorative crowning element atop a tall case piece, often triangular or scrolled.

**Pigeonholes.** The small open compartments in a fitted desk interior.

**Primary wood.** The good show wood on visible surfaces. See secondary wood.

**Quartersawn.** Lumber sawn radially to show the ray flecks, prized in Golden Oak and Mission oak.

**Rule joint.** The shaped, interlocking hinge edge between a tabletop and its drop leaf.

**Secondary wood.** The cheaper wood used inside, on drawer sides, bottoms, and backs. The mix is a dating clue.

**Splat.** The central flat upright in a chair back.

**Stile and rail.** The vertical (stile) and horizontal (rail) members of a frame or frame-and-panel.

**Stretcher.** A horizontal brace connecting legs.

**Tambour.** A flexible cover of thin slats glued to a canvas backing, used on roll-top desks.

**Till.** The small lidded compartment inside the upper edge of a blanket chest.

**Turning.** A part shaped on a lathe: legs, spindles, posts. Barley-twist, ring, ball, spool, and bobbin are turning styles.

**Veneer.** A thin layer of show wood glued over a cheaper substrate.
