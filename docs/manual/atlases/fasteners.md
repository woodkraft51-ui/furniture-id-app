## Nails

| Nail | How to tell it | Date it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Hand-wrought** | Irregular, tapered on all four sides, with a hand-hammered rose or L head; no two alike | before about 1800 |
| **Cut (square)** | Machine-sheared from a sheet, rectangular and tapered on two sides only, with a blunt tip; head often slightly irregular | about 1790 to 1890 |
| **Wire (round)** | Round shank, perfectly uniform, with a round flat head and a sharp point | common after about 1890 |

Reading nails: hand-wrought says early, cut nails say roughly the nineteenth century, wire nails say late nineteenth century or later. Remember a nail dates the **board it is in**, not always the whole piece, since repairs add later nails to old furniture.

## Screws

| Screw | How to tell it | Date it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Hand-made** | Off-center slot, uneven threads, a blunt unthreaded tip, file marks on the head | before about 1846 |
| **Machine-made, slotted** | Even threads, centered single slot, but still a gimlet (pointed) tip after about 1846 | most of the 1800s into the 1900s |
| **Phillips (cross slot)** | A cross-shaped recess | after the 1930s, a modern tell |

The most useful single screw fact: **a Phillips screw did not exist before the 1930s.** A Phillips screw in a structural joint means that part is modern, full stop.

## How to use this in the field
- A wire nail or a Phillips screw anywhere structural caps the earliest possible date.
- Cut nails and slotted screws keep the nineteenth century open.
- Hand-wrought nails and hand-made screws point genuinely early, but confirm with the rest of the construction, since both were also used in early repairs.
