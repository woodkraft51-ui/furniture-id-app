# Atlas: Hardware and Casters

> Part III reference. Pulls, knobs, hinges, and casters by period, with the one warning that
> matters most.

## The warning, first: hardware is the most-replaced part

Pulls, knobs, and casters are swapped more than any other part of a piece. A later caster or a shiny modern pull can fool you into dating the whole thing too late or too early. **Weigh hardware lightly, and look for the evidence of earlier hardware:** extra holes, shadow outlines, and unfilled mounting marks. (We watched our own software get fooled by a replaced caster, which is exactly why this warning leads the atlas.)

## Pulls and knobs

| Hardware | Look | Period it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Bail pull, willow or rococo backplate** | A swinging handle on a pierced brass plate | eighteenth century and Colonial Revival copies |
| **Oval or round stamped brass, Hepplewhite** | Light stamped brass with a bail | Federal, c. 1790 to 1820, and revivals |
| **Pressed-glass or wood knob** | Turned wood knob, or a faceted glass knob | Victorian country and Empire, glass into the 1900s |
| **Cast Victorian pull, teardrop or ring** | Heavy cast brass or iron, ornate | mid-to-late Victorian |
| **Eastlake stamped or cast** | Geometric, incised, angular | 1875 to 1900 |
| **Oak-era bail and backplate** | Stamped brass or brassed steel, often with lions or florals | Golden Oak, 1890 to 1915 |
| **Bakelite or chrome pull** | Plastic or streamlined metal | Art Deco and later, 1925 onward |

## Hinges and escutcheons

`[VISUAL: diagrams/hinges-escutcheons.svg]`

- **HL and H hinges**, exposed on cupboard doors, read early.
- **Butterfly hinges**, exposed and shaped like a bow tie, are an early cabinet hinge of the 18th and 19th centuries.
- **Butt hinges**, mortised and hidden, become standard; machine-made uniform butt hinges are factory era.
- **Escutcheons** (keyhole plates) follow the same style chronology as pulls.

## Casters

`[VISUAL: diagrams/casters-types.svg]`

| Caster | Look | Period it points to |
|---|---|---|
| **Porcelain wheel** | White or cream ceramic wheel in a metal cup | c. 1860 to 1900 |
| **Cast-iron wheel** | Plain dark metal wheel | c. 1880 to 1920 |
| **Brass cup and leather or wood wheel** | Earlier, finer | Federal and early Victorian, better pieces |
| **Rubber or composition wheel** | Black rubber or composite | after about 1900, and very often a replacement |

## How to use this in the field
- Read hardware style for a hint, never a verdict.
- Hunt for extra or shadow holes that betray earlier, original hardware.
- Distrust a single late caster as a dating clue; it is usually a swap.
