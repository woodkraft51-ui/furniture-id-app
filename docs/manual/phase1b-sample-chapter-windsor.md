# Windsor Chair
*also called: spindle chair · sack-back · bow-back · comb-back · fan-back · hoop-back · "Colonial" chair*

---

## Rule it out in 10

**Look at the seat and where the legs go.**
A Windsor has a **solid carved wooden plank seat**, with the **legs socketed straight up into the underside**: no apron, no frame, no rails under the seat.

> Upholstered, rush, or cane seat? **Not a Windsor.**
> Legs joined to an apron or a square frame? **Not a Windsor.**
> Plank seat, with legs driven straight into it and splayed out? **Short list. Keep going.**

---

## What it is
The Windsor isn't a *style*, it's a **construction system**. Everything about it hangs
off one idea: a thick solid-wood **plank seat** that serves as the structural hub. The
**back spindles socket down into the seat**, the **legs socket up into it**, and the legs
**splay** outward for stability. The back is built from turned spindles and a shaped
crest, **never** the flat rail-and-stile frame of an ordinary chair.

It's one of the great American forms. Philadelphia, New England, and New York were major
centers, with the golden age running **c. 1740 to 1820**. But here's the field reality for
*this* guide's era: genuine 18th-century Windsors are largely museum-and-auction pieces.
**What you'll actually meet from 1840 onward is 19th-century continuation and, by a wide
margin, Colonial Revival reproductions (c. 1870 to today).** So the money question is
almost never "is it a Windsor?" It's **"is it period, or a revival reproduction?"** That
call is the rest of this page.

---

## On the short list? Confirm it

**The defining features** (you want most of these):
- Solid plank seat, usually **saddle-shaped** (carved hollows for the sitter)
- Back spindles **socketed into the seat**, not mortised into a rail
- Legs **socketed into the seat underside**, splayed
- **Turned** legs, stretchers, and posts
- A back type from the family below, but **never** a rail-and-stile frame

## Date it

| Tool marks | Fasteners/joints | Wood | Finish | Wear |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hand drawknife and spokeshave **facets**, slight irregularity, point period. Uniform, sanded-smooth, identical parts point machine or repro. | **Wedged through-tenon** sockets, hand-fitted, point period. Modern screws, dowels, glue-only, or perfectly round machine sockets point revival. | **Multiple woods chosen by job** (pine seat, maple or birch legs, oak or hickory bent parts) point period craft. One uniform wood throughout points factory repro. | **Original milk or oil paint** (Windsors were almost always painted) with honest layered wear is period gold. Clear-stained "natural wood" or thick modern poly points revival. | Genuine **underside and stretcher wear**, settled splay, old shrinkage point period. Crisp, even, no real wear points recent. |

**The headline call:** *period (pre-1900) versus Colonial Revival reproduction (1870 to present).*
No single clue decides it: **stack the layers.** Three or more pointing "hand and old" means likely
period; machine tells anywhere mean treat it as revival until proven otherwise.

See the Fastener Atlas (wedged joints), Wood Atlas (multi-wood), Finishes Atlas (milk paint), and Joinery Atlas (socketed through-tenon).

## Do not confuse it with

- **Ladderback:** horizontal slats between two rear posts; a real (often rush) seat in a frame. No plank seat, no socketed spindles, so not a Windsor.
- **Spindle-back chair (generic):** "spindle-back" just describes a look. A Windsor **requires** the plank seat and socketed construction; spindles alone aren't enough.
- **"Windsor-style" reproduction:** looks the part but shows modern machining, fasteners, finish, and tool marks. This is the one that actually fools people (see Date it).

---

## What it is worth, in plain terms
The spread is enormous and **hinges entirely on period versus repro.** A documented 18th to early-19th-century
painted Windsor is a serious antique; a 20th-century Colonial Revival example is decorator furniture.
*Get the dating right before you price it.*

## Watch for
- **Revival repros sold as period:** the number-one trap. Machine-uniform parts and modern finish.
- **Stripped paint:** refinishing an original-painted Windsor down to bare wood *destroys* value. Original paint is the prize.
- **Replaced or re-pegged legs, married tops and bases:** check that wear, wood, and patina agree across the whole chair.
