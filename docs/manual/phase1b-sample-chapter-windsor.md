## ⚡ RULE IT OUT IN 10
`[BOX, outer rail, top — bold, the first thing the eye hits]`

**Look at the seat and where the legs go.**
A Windsor has a **solid carved wooden plank seat**, with the **legs socketed straight up into the underside** — no apron, no frame, no rails under the seat.

> ❌ Upholstered, rush, or cane seat? → **Not a Windsor.**
> ❌ Legs joined to an apron or a square frame? → **Not a Windsor.**
> ✅ Plank seat + legs driven straight into it, splayed out? → **Short list. Keep going.**

## What it is
The Windsor isn't a *style* — it's a **construction system**. Everything about it hangs
off one idea: a thick solid-wood **plank seat** that serves as the structural hub. The
**back spindles socket down into the seat**, the **legs socket up into it**, and the legs
**splay** outward for stability. The back is built from turned spindles and a shaped
crest — **never** the flat rail-and-stile frame of an ordinary chair.

It's one of the great American forms — Philadelphia, New England, and New York were major
centers, with the golden age running **c. 1740–1820**. But here's the field reality for
*this* guide's era: genuine 18th-century Windsors are largely museum-and-auction pieces.
**What you'll actually meet from 1840 onward is 19th-century continuation and — by a wide
margin — Colonial Revival reproductions (c. 1870 to today).** So the money question is
almost never "is it a Windsor?" It's **"is it period, or a revival reproduction?"** That
call is the rest of this page.

## 🔍 ON THE SHORT LIST? CONFIRM IT

**The defining features** (you want most of these):
- Solid plank seat, usually **saddle-shaped** (carved hollows for the sitter)
- Back spindles **socketed into the seat** — not mortised into a rail
- Legs **socketed into the seat underside**, splayed
- **Turned** legs, stretchers, and posts
- A back type from the family below — but **never** a rail-and-stile frame

## DATE IT
`[STRIP — Verdigris-keyed; horizontal band, one icon-cell per layer]`

| 🪚 Tool marks | 🔩 Fasteners/joints | 🪵 Wood | 🎨 Finish | 〰 Wear |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hand: drawknife & spokeshave **facets**, slight irregularity → period. Uniform, sanded-smooth, identical parts → machine/repro. | **Wedged through-tenon** sockets, hand-fitted → period. Modern screws, dowels, glue-only, perfectly round machine sockets → revival. | **Multiple woods chosen by job** (pine seat, maple/birch legs, oak/hickory bent parts) → period craft. One uniform wood throughout → factory repro. | **Original milk/oil paint** (Windsors were almost always painted) with honest layered wear → period gold. Clear-stained "natural wood," thick modern poly → revival. | Genuine **underside & stretcher wear**, settled splay, old shrinkage → period. Crisp, even, no real wear → recent. |

**The headline call:** *period (pre-1900) vs. Colonial Revival reproduction (1870–present).*
No single clue decides it — **stack the layers.** Three or more pointing "hand/old" = likely
period; machine tells anywhere = treat as revival until proven otherwise.

## DON'T CONFUSE IT WITH
`[COMPARISON PLATE: paired line-art silhouettes on tan blocks, the distinguishing tell circled in Oxblood]`

- **Ladderback** — horizontal slats between two rear posts; a real (often rush) seat in a frame. *No plank seat, no socketed spindles.* → not a Windsor.
- **Spindle-back chair (generic)** — "spindle-back" just describes a look. Windsor **requires** the plank seat + socketed construction. Spindles alone aren't enough.
- **"Windsor-style" reproduction** — *looks* the part but shows modern machining, fasteners, finish, and tool marks. (This is the one that actually fools people — see DATE IT.)

## 💲 VALUE
`[BADGE — Oxblood-keyed, compact]`
Spread is enormous and **hinges entirely on period vs. repro.** A documented 18th–early-19th-c.
painted Windsor is a serious antique; a 20th-c. Colonial Revival example is decorator furniture.
*Get the dating right before you price it.* `[per-condition ranges pulled from engine valuation in production layout]`

## ⚠ WATCH FOR
`[CALLOUT — Oxblood]`
- **Revival repros sold as period** — the #1 trap. Machine-uniform parts + modern finish.
- **Stripped paint** — refinishing an original-painted Windsor down to bare wood *destroys* value. Original paint is the prize.
- **Replaced/re-pegged legs, married tops & bases** — check that wear, wood, and patina agree across the whole chair.
