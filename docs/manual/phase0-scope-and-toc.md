## Selection criteria for the "right 40"
1. **In scope** — American antique, ~1850–1940 (matches the app). Modern forms excluded.
2. **Commonly encountered** — what a thrift-store / estate-sale / garage-sale hunter
   actually runs into. (Note: not by alias-count — many modern forms have high alias
   counts; many common antiques have few. Curated by real-world frequency.)
3. **Rich authored source** — has enough canonical prose to make a strong chapter.

## Explicitly EXCLUDED (out of scope — note for owner)
Modern forms in the engine but wrong for an *antique* guide: recliner, bean bag,
papasan, butterfly/BKF chair, cocktail/coffee table, mid-century credenza, acrylic
pieces. (These belong to a future "Mid-Century" edition, not this one.)

# Phase 0 — Scope & Table of Contents (owner-approved structure 2026-06-08)

> **Title:** *Field Guide to American Furniture Identification, 1840–1940*
> **Subtitle / ad line:** *Spotting, Dating & Valuing the Antiques You'll Actually Find — Victorian to Art Deco*

## Three-part architecture (owner-directed Q5)
Walk the reader through the *process*, then the *forms*, then give them the *skills* to
actually do it. "My advice doesn't matter if they can't identify a wood or a fastener."

- **PART I — THE METHOD** (front; the "how to think")
  - How to use this guide
  - The field process (mirrors the engine): **Look → Read the construction → Date by layers
    → Compare to look-alikes → Estimate value → Call it.**
  - Style as the *weakest* evidence + **Period-style timeline 1840–1940**
    (late Empire/Gothic Revival · Rococo & Renaissance Revival · Eastlake/Aesthetic ·
    Golden Oak · Mission/Arts & Crafts · Art Deco/Waterfall), with a brief "earlier
    styles you might glimpse" nod (Federal/Chippendale) — not full colonial coverage.
- **PART II — THE FORMS** (middle; the "what you'll find") — the 30 form chapters + Clocks.
- **PART III — FIELD REFERENCE ATLASES** (back; the "how to actually see it" — the skill-builders)
  - **Wood Species ID** (grain/pore/color plates: walnut, oak, mahogany, cherry, maple, pine, poplar, gum…)
  - **Fastener Types ID** (hand-wrought → cut → wire nails; slotted vs. Phillips screws; the date each pins)
  - **Joinery Types ID** (hand vs. machine dovetail; mortise-and-tenon; dowel; rabbet; what each dates)
  - **Hardware & Casters ID** (pull/knob chronology; porcelain → rubber casters; "usually replaced")
  - **Finishes ID** (shellac/varnish vs. lacquer vs. modern poly; refinish tells)
  - **Glossary** of terms used throughout.
  - *These atlases are the most visual section and the direct seed for the future teaching app.*

## SCOPE DECISION — era set to 1840–1940 (owner delegated the call to Claude, 2026-06-08)
Changed from the initially-floated 1600–1940 to **1840–1940** as the *market-smart* scope:
- **Matches what buyers actually find** (thrift/estate/garage = Victorian→Deco; true colonial is rare in the wild).
- **Honest to the source** (~1850–1940-centric; 1840 is a small, defensible stretch — Gothic Revival/late Empire).
- **Clean 100-year span** = strong cover/ad hook ("A Century of American Furniture").
- **Over-delivers vs. over-promises** → better reviews / word-of-mouth than a hollow "1600."
- **Early end not lost:** the dating-method chapters still teach pre-1840 tells as a *bonus*
  ("if you find something older, here's how you'd know") — without promising colonial depth.
Owner may override back to strict 1850–1940 or to 1600 if desired; 1840–1940 is Claude's market recommendation, accepted by owner.

## (Original) recommended 40 — superseded by the 30 above, kept for reference

### Front matter — the evergreen value (NOT counted in the 40; the real differentiator)
These cross-cutting "how to read any piece" chapters are what make the guide worth
buying and what carry over verbatim into the future teaching mode:
- **Ch 1 — How dating works:** the layered-evidence idea (construction beats style).
- **Ch 2 — Fasteners:** hand-wrought vs. cut vs. wire nails; slotted vs. Phillips screws (the post-1934 tell).
- **Ch 3 — Joinery & tool marks:** hand vs. machine dovetails; circular-saw arcs (post-1830); mortise-and-tenon.
- **Ch 4 — Wood & veneer:** species chronology; solid vs. veneer vs. plywood eras.
- **Ch 5 — Hardware & casters:** pull/knob chronology; porcelain → rubber casters (and "it's usually replaced").
- **Ch 6 — Finish:** shellac/varnish vs. modern coatings; refinish tells.
- **Ch 7 — Style as the *weakest* evidence:** why style dates loosely; revival waves.
- **Ch 8 — Maker marks, labels & the gotchas:** owner/mover scrawls ≠ maker dates.

## Scope judgment calls — OWNER DECIDES at touch-base
1. **Clocks?** Tall-case (grandfather) + shelf/mantel clocks are *extremely* common and
   giftable, but are arguably not "furniture." Options: (a) add a short "Clocks" chapter
   (high gift appeal), (b) leave out, keep it pure furniture, (c) future edition. **Rec: add a short chapter — strong gift-market draw.**
2. **Lighting?** Kerosene/Argand/student lamps, chandeliers, sconces are in the engine but
   are a different category. **Rec: leave out of FD edition** (own future title).
3. **Pressed-back / spindle "kitchen" chairs** — hugely common, but no clean dedicated
   form (route under chair forms). **Rec: fold into the Windsor/seating chapter as a variant call-out.**
4. **Title** — "*A Field Guide*" vs "*Field Identification Guide*" vs other. **Rec: lead with "Field Guide."**
5. **Per-chapter template** — proposed: *What it is · How to recognize it (silhouette + defining feature) · How to date it (the tells, by layer) · How it differs from look-alikes · What it's worth (range) · Buying/selling tips.* (This is the pattern Phase 1's sample chapter will lock.)
