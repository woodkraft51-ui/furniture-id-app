Date & style detail

Regional context ▸

American Arts & Crafts (“Mission”) production centered on Grand Rapids, Michigan and Syracuse, New York (Gustav Stickley). Quarter-sawn white oak paired with hammered-copper hardware is the signature of mid-grade lines sold through Sears, Roebuck and Montgomery Ward catalogs in the 1920s.

This trestle library table reads as catalog-grade rather than a named-maker piece — broad distribution, so regional attribution is loose.

History & use ▸

Library tables were a parlor staple from roughly 1900–1930 — a central surface for writing, reading lamps, and display before the built-in desk became common. The single shallow drawer held stationery.

Demand for the form faded through the 1930s as homes shrank and styles turned to Colonial Revival.

How it differs from similar pieces ▸

vs. a true Stickley table: higher grade, branded (decal or paper label), through-tenons pinned and visible at the legs. This piece has hidden joinery and no mark — a step below.

vs. a Colonial Revival writing table: those use thinner stock, mahogany or veneer, and cabriole or tapered legs rather than a heavy oak trestle.

vs. a 1950s reproduction: repros show power-tool uniformity, plywood panels, and Phillips screws — absent here.

Key supporting evidence

  • • Quarter-sawn white oak with prominent ray-fleck figure on top and apron.
  • • Hammered-copper drawer pull with a hand-finished, slightly irregular face.
  • • Trestle base with mortise-and-tenon joinery; no visible nails or screws on show surfaces.
  • • Thin, amber early-lacquer finish consistent with 1920s factory output.

Worth a second look

Hardware may be a replacement. The drawer pull shows less wear than the surrounding wood. If original, it supports the 1920s date; if swapped later, the hardware signal weakens and the date leans on Form alone.

Next best evidence to check

Look underneath. Photograph the underside of the top and the inside/back of the drawer for a paper label, branded mark, or stencil. Check the back of the drawer pull for a maker's stamp — a mark here could move this from catalog-grade into named-maker territory and reset the resale ceiling.

Negotiating & selling tips

  • Lead the listing with “quarter-sawn oak” and “Arts & Crafts” — both are search terms buyers use.
  • State the finish is original; for this style that's a selling point, not a flaw.
  • Local pickup beats shipping — oak tables are heavy and freight eats the margin.
  • If a buyer pushes on price, the loose joints are your honest lever — price the “as-is” lane and let them weigh the repair.
